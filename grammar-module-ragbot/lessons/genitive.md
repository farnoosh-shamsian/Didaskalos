## Introduction to the Genitive Case

In ancient Greek the genitive is expressed by adding a fixed suffix to the noun’s stem. The suffix that is used depends on the declension to which the noun belongs and on whether the form is singular or plural.

**How the genitive is built**

1. **Find the stem** – strip the nominative ending (‑α, ‑η, ‑ος, ‑ον, etc.) to obtain the base.
2. **Add the appropriate genitive suffix** – each declension has a characteristic set of endings for singular and plural.
3. **Keep the original accent** – the accent does not move onto the added suffix; it remains on the same syllable as in the nominative unless the word is an enclitic, in which case the preceding word can affect the accent.

---

### First‑declension (‑α / ‑η stems, mainly feminine)

| Number   | Genitive ending      | Example                      |
| -------- | -------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Singular | **‑ης** (or **‑ας**) | τῆς γυναικ‑ης “of the woman” |
| Plural   | **‑ῶν**              | τῶν γυναικ‑ῶν “of the women” |

The long vowel of the ending forces the accent onto the penult of the whole word.

---

### Second‑declension (‑ο‑ stems, masculine and neuter)

| Number   | Genitive ending | Masculine example           | Neuter example                |
| -------- | --------------- | --------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Singular | **‑ου**         | τοῦ φίλ‑ου “of the friend”  | τοῦ παιδ‑ου “of the child”    |
| Plural   | **‑ων**         | τῶν φίλ‑ων “of the friends” | τῶν παιδ‑ων “of the children” |

The accent normally stays where it was in the nominative form.

---

### Third‑declension (consonant‑stem nouns, mixed gender)

The ending varies with the shape of the stem.

| Stem type                                                      | Singular ending | Plural ending | Example                                                              |
| -------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------- | ------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Masculine/feminine with a long vowel or diphthong in the stem  | **‑ος**         | **‑ων**       | τοῦ δίκαι‑ου “of the just person”, τῶν δίκαι‑ων “of the just people” |
| Masculine/feminine with a short vowel before the ending (rare) | **‑ις**         | **‑ων**       | (illustrative) τοῦ παιδ‑ις “of the child”                            |
| Neuter with a consonant‑stem                                   | **‑ος**         | **‑ων**       | τοῦ πράγ‑ου “of the deed”, τῶν πράγ‑ων “of the deeds”                |

When the stem ends in a long vowel, the genitive follows the same **‑ου / ‑ων** pattern as the second declension; many other third‑declension nouns keep the **‑ος / ‑ων** pattern.

---

### Summary of the pattern

| Declension | Singular suffix | Plural suffix |
| ---------- | --------------- | ------------- |
| 1st        | ‑ης / ‑ας       | ‑ῶν           |
| 2nd        | ‑ου             | ‑ων           |
| 3rd        | ‑ος (or ‑ις)    | ‑ων           |

Thus, the morphology of the genitive case in ancient Greek is a systematic addition of a suffix that is determined by the noun’s declension and number, with the accent remaining on the original stem syllable. This yields forms such as **τῆς γυναικ‑ης**, **τοῦ φίλ‑ου**, **τῶν γυναικ‑ῶν**, **τῶν φίλ‑ων**, **τοῦ δίκαι‑ου**, and **τῶν δίκαι‑ων**.
