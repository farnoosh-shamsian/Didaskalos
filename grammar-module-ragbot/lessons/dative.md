## Introduction to the Dative Case

The dative case marks the indirect object, the recipient, or the “to/for” relation. Its form is created by attaching a case‑ending to the noun’s stem; the shape of that ending depends on the declension to which the noun belongs.

---

### 1. First‑declension (‑ᾱ / ‑η stems)

| Gender           | Singular ending | Example                                     | Plural ending     | Example                                         |
| ---------------- | --------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ----------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Feminine         | ‑ᾱ‑ι / ‑η‑ι     | _γλῶσ‑σῃ_ → **γλῶσῃ** “to/for the tongue”   | ‑α‑ις (or ‑α‑ισι) | _γλῶσ‑σαις_ → **γλώσσαις** “to/for the tongues” |
| Masculine (rare) | ‑ᾱ‑ι            | _νεαν‑ι_ → **νεανί** “to/for the young man” | ‑α‑ις             | _νεαν‑αίς_ → **νεαναίς** “to/for the young men” |

_Morphology_ – The stem ends in a long vowel (‑ᾱ or ‑η). In the singular the long‑i **‑ι** is added directly after that vowel, giving a long‑i ending (‑ᾱι, ‑ηι). In the plural the vowel is followed by **‑α‑ις**, producing a short‑i ending that is pronounced as a single syllable.

---

### 2. Second‑declension (‑ο stems, including “‑ο‑stem” adjectives)

| Gender             | Singular ending | Example                                            | Plural ending | Example                                                  |
| ------------------ | --------------- | -------------------------------------------------- | ------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| Masculine / neuter | ‑ῳ              | _φίλ‑ῳ_ → **τῷ φίλῳ** “to/for the friend”          | ‑οις          | _φίλ‑οις_ → **τοῖς φίλοις** “to/for the friends”         |
| Feminine (‑ος)     | ‑ῳ              | _δίκαι‑ῳ_ → **τῷ δικαίῳ** “to/for the just person” | ‑οις          | _δίκαι‑οις_ → **τοῖς δικαίοις** “to/for the just people” |

_Morphology_ – The stem ends in **‑ο** (or **‑ε** for some neuters). The dative singular adds the long‑i **‑ῳ**, while the plural adds **‑οις**. The original accent of the noun is retained as far as Greek phonological rules allow.

---

### 3. Third‑declension (mixed‑stem nouns)

Because third‑declension stems vary (consonantal, i‑stems, u‑stems, etc.), several patterns occur. The most frequent are:

| Stem type      | Singular ending | Example                                   | Plural ending | Example                                         |
| -------------- | --------------- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Consonant‑stem | ‑ι              | _πόλε‑ι_ → **τῷ πόλει** “to/for the city” | ‑σι           | _πόλε‑σι_ → **τοῖς πόλεσι** “to/for the cities” |
| i‑stem         | ‑ι              | _γῆ‑ι_ → **τῇ γῇ** “to/for the earth”     | ‑σι           | _γῆ‑σι_ → **τοῖς γῆσι** “to/for the lands”      |
| u‑stem         | ‑ῳ              | _ναῦ‑ῳ_ → **τῷ ναῦῳ** “to/for the ship”   | ‑οις          | _ναῦ‑οις_ → **τοῖς ναῦοις** “to/for the ships”  |

_Morphology_ – The stem may undergo a minor adjustment (often a lengthening or a consonant change) before the dative suffix is attached. The default suffixes are **‑ι** for the singular and **‑σι** for the plural; u‑stems follow the second‑declension pattern **‑ῳ / ‑οις**.

---

### General principles that apply to all declensions

1. **Accent preservation** – The noun’s original accent stays on the same syllable whenever the addition of the dative ending does not force a shift. An acute on the penult of the nominative typically remains on the penult in the dative; an acute on the antepenult may move forward when the ending is added.
2. **Length of the ultima** – Dative endings themselves are long (**‑ῳ, ‑οις**) or short (**‑ι, ‑σι**). This length influences accent placement and the behavior of any following enclitic.
3. **Enclitic interaction** – When a dative form is followed by an enclitic (e.g., the verb _εἰμί_), the enclitic receives no accent if the preceding word ends in a long ultima; otherwise the enclitic may acquire an accent on its final syllable.

---

### Summary of the core endings

| Declension          | Singular ending | Plural ending            |
| ------------------- | --------------- | ------------------------ |
| First               | ‑ᾱ‑ι / ‑η‑ι     | ‑α‑ις                    |
| Second              | ‑ῳ              | ‑οις                     |
| Third (most common) | ‑ι              | ‑σι (u‑stems: ‑ῳ / ‑οις) |

These endings attach directly to the noun’s stem, producing the dative forms that express “to” or “for” the noun in Ancient Greek.
