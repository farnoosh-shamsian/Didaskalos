# Lesson: The Present‑Indicative‑Active (‑ω) in Ancient Greek  

*Target level: beginner → intermediate*  
*Prerequisite: knowledge of the Greek alphabet and basic noun declension.*

---

## 1. What the Present‑Indicative‑Active Means  

| Term | Meaning in English | Function in a sentence |
|------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Present** | Action happening **now** or a **general/habitual** truth. | “I write”, “the sun rises”. |
| **Indicative** | The **statement mood** – the speaker presents the action as real or factual. | Not a wish, command, or doubt. |
| **Active** | The **subject** performs the action (as opposed to passive, where the subject receives it). | “He loves **her**” (active) vs. “She is loved **by him**” (passive). |
| **‑ω** | The **first‑person singular** ending of the present‑indicative‑active for most verbs. It is the “dictionary” form you will see in lexica (e.g., **λύω** “I loosen”). |

Thus, the present‑indicative‑active answers the question **“What does the subject do (now)?”** and is the most common verb form in narrative and exposition.

---

## 2. The Regular Paradigm  

Ancient Greek verbs fall into three major **conjugation classes**:

1. **Thematic (vowel‑stem) verbs** – stem ends in a vowel (‑α, ‑ε, ‑ο, ‑ι, ‑υ).  
2. **Contract verbs** – a vowel‑stem that contracts with the personal endings.  
3. **A‑thematic (consonant‑stem) verbs** – stem ends in a consonant (often **‑ν**, **‑ρ**, **‑σ**, **‑τ**, **‑κ**, **‑γ**).  

Below are the **full present‑indicative‑active endings** (the same for all three classes, but the stem may change before them).

### 2.1 Endings (Active Voice)

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | ‑ω | ‑ομεν |
| 2nd | ‑εις / ‑ῃς* | ‑ετε |
| 3rd | ‑ει(ν) / ‑ει* | ‑ουσι(ν) / ‑ουσιν* |

\* The **‑ει** form is the older (Attic) ending; **‑ειν** is the later (Koine) form.  
\* In poetry the **‑ει** may appear without the final **‑ν**.

### 2.2 Thematic Verb Example: **λύω** “to loosen, to release”

|          | Singular | Plural |
|----------|----------|--------|
| 1st      | **λύω**   | **λύομεν** |
| 2nd      | **λύεις** | **λύετε** |
| 3rd      | **λύει**  | **λύουσι(ν)** |

*Note:* The thematic vowel **‑ο‑** appears before the endings in the plural (‑ομεν, ‑ετε, ‑ουσι).

### 2.3 Contract Verb Example: **φιλέω** → **φιλοῦ** “to love”

|          | Singular | Plural |
|----------|----------|--------|
| 1st      | **φιλοῦ**   | **φιλοῦμεν** |
| 2nd      | **φιλεῖς**  | **φιλεῖτε** |
| 3rd      | **φιλεῖ**   | **φιλοῦσι(ν)** |

*Explanation:*  
- **‑εω** → **‑οῦ** in the 1st sg. (contraction of **‑ε‑ + ‑ω**).  
- **‑ε‑** + **‑εις** → **‑εῖς** (2nd sg.).  
- **‑ε‑** + **‑ει** → **‑εῖ** (3rd sg.).  
- Plural retains the thematic **‑ο‑** (‑οῦμεν, ‑εῖτε, ‑οῦσι).

### 2.4 A‑thematic Verb Example: **πράττω** “to do, to practice”

|          | Singular | Plural |
|----------|----------|--------|
| 1st      | **πράττω**   | **πράττομεν** |
| 2nd      | **πράττεις** | **πράττετε** |
| 3rd      | **πράττει**  | **πράττουσι(ν)** |

*Explanation:* The stem ends in **‑ττ‑**; the endings are attached directly (no thematic vowel).

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation & Example |
|------|-----------------------|
| **No augment** | The augment (ἐ‑) is used only in the **aorist** and **imperf**. Present tense never adds it. |
| **Vowel contraction** | When a vowel‑stem meets a vowel ending, they contract. <br>• **‑εω + ‑ω → ‑οῦ** (φιλε‑ω → φιλοῦ). <br>• **‑αω + ‑ω → ‑ῶ** (διδά‑ω → διδά̄ω). |
| **Thematic vowel ‑ο‑ in the plural** | All present‑indicative‑active plurals (except 2nd sg.) contain **‑ο‑** before the ending (‑ομεν, ‑ετε, ‑ουσι). |
| **Spurious diphthongs** | In some verbs the diphthong **‑ει** or **‑ου** is “spurious” (historically from a long vowel). They behave like long vowels for quantity. |
| **Irregular verbs** | A few very common verbs have irregular stems or endings. |
| • **εἰμί** “to be” | 1sg εἰμί, 2sg εἶ, 3sg ἐστί(ν), 1pl ἐσμέν, 2pl ἐστέ, 3pl εἰσίν. |
| • **εἶμι** “to go” | 1sg εἶμι, 2sg ἔχεις, 3sg ἔστι, 1pl ἔσμεν, 2pl ἔστε, 3pl εἰσίν (often replaced by **ἔρχομαι**). |
| • **ἔχω** “to have, hold” | 1sg ἔχω, 2sg ἔχεις, 3sg ἔχει, 1pl ἔχομεν, 2pl ἔχετε, 3pl ἔχουσι(ν). |
| **Middle‑voice forms** | The same endings are used for the **middle** voice, but the meaning changes (e.g., **λύομαι** “I am loosed / I loosen for myself”). This lesson focuses on the **active** voice only. |
| **Dialectal variation** | In Ionic and later Koine, the 3rd‑person plural ending **‑ουσι(ν)** may appear as **‑ουσι** (no final **‑ν**). |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (present‑indicative‑active) | Transliteration | English |
|-----------------------------------|----------------|---------|
| **Λύω τὸν δεσμὸν.** | Lýō ton desmòn. | I loosen the bond. |
| **Φιλεῖς τὸν ἥλιον.** | Phileîs tòn hḗlion. | You love the sun. |
| **Πράττουμεν τὰ ἔργα.** | Práttoumen tà érga. | We do the deeds. |
| **Ἀκούει τὸν λόγον.** | Akoúei tòn lógon. | He/she hears the word. |
| **Ἐσθίετε τὰ ἄρτα.** | Esthíete tà ánta. | You (pl.) eat the breads. |
| **Ἐλθόντες εἰσίν.** *(aorist participle + present of εἰμί)* | Elthóntes eisin. | They have come (are) [present of “to be”]. |

*Note:* The verb **εἰμί** is irregular; it is shown here to illustrate a present‑indicative‑active that does **not** end in ‑ω.

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Narrative prose** – The present is used for vivid, “historical present” narration (e.g., “He draws his sword and strikes”).  
2. **General truths** – Statements of fact or habitual action (e.g., **ὁ ἥλιος ἀνίσταται** “the sun rises”).  
3. **Instructions & recipes** – Often the present is used for step‑by‑step directions (e.g., **προσθέτεις ὕδωρ** “add water”).  
4. **Poetry** – The present may appear with shortened forms (‑ει, ‑ου) for metrical reasons.  
5. **Dialogue** – In drama, characters frequently speak in the present to convey immediacy.

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** | The present‑indicative‑active derives from the PIE *‑ó‑* suffix (the “present” suffix) attached to the verb root. The **‑ω** ending reflects the PIE *‑ō* (first‑person singular). |
| **Early Greek** | In Mycenaean Greek the present forms appear with the same endings, though the spelling uses Linear B signs (e.g., *wo‑* for *-ō*). |
| **Contraction** | The phenomenon of vowel contraction (e.g., **‑εω + ‑ω → ‑οῦ**) developed in the Classical period as a phonological simplification. |
| **Middle‑voice emergence** | Originally a reflexive or mediopassive voice, the middle uses the same endings but a different set of meanings; the active voice remained the default for transitive actions. |
| **Later Greek (Koine & Byzantine)** | The **‑ει** ending for 2nd/3rd sg. and the loss of the final **‑ν** in 3rd‑pl. became standard, reflecting a general trend toward simplification. |

### Take‑away  

- The **present‑indicative‑active** is the “default” verb form for stating what **someone does** in the present.  
- Memorise the **six endings** (‑ω, ‑εις, ‑ει, ‑ομεν, ‑ετε, ‑ουσι) and watch for **contractions** in contract verbs.  
- Be aware of the **few irregular verbs** (εἰμί, εἶμι, ἔχω) that do not follow the regular pattern.  

With practice, you will recognize the present‑indicative‑active instantly and be able to read and translate Greek prose and poetry with confidence. Happy studying!
