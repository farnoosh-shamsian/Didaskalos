# Lesson: The Aorist Indicative Active of **‑μι** Verbs  
*(e.g., ἵστημι “to stand”, τίθημι “to place”, φῆμι “to say”)*  

---

## 1. What the Aorist Indicative Active Is  

| Term | Meaning in Greek grammar | Typical English equivalent |
|------|--------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Aorist** | A past‑time aspect that presents an action as a simple, completed whole, without reference to its duration or repetition. | “did / went / said” (simple past) |
| **Indicative** | The “real” mood – statements of fact, questions, or commands that are not hypothetical. | – |
| **Active** | The subject performs the action. | – |
| **‑μι verbs** | Verbs whose **present‑stem** ends in **‑μι** (e.g., ἵστημι, τίθημι, φῆμι). In the aorist they behave like **first‑aorist** verbs: they add **‑σ‑** before the personal endings. | – |

So the **aorist indicative active** of a ‑μι verb tells us **“someone simply did X”** in the past, with the subject actively carrying out the action.

---

## 2. Forms & Patterns  

Below is the full **paradigm** for a regular first‑aorist ‑μι verb, illustrated with **ἵστημι** “to stand”.  
(The same pattern applies to most other ‑μι verbs such as **τίθημι** “to place”.)

| Person / Number | Form | Transliteration | Gloss |
|-----------------|------|----------------|-------|
| **1st sg.** | ἔστησα | *éstēsa* | I stood |
| **2nd sg.** | ἔστησας | *éstēsas* | you (sg.) stood |
| **3rd sg.** | ἔστησεν | *éstēsen* | he/she/it stood |
| **1st pl.** | ἐστήσαμεν | *estḗsamen* | we stood |
| **2nd pl.** | ἐστήσατε | *estḗsate* | you (pl.) stood |
| **3rd pl.** | ἔστησαν | *éstēsan* | they stood |

### How the forms are built  

1. **Augment** – a prefixed **ε‑** (e‑) marks past time.  
2. **Aorist stem** – take the present stem (‑μι) and replace it with **‑σ‑** (the aorist marker).  
3. **Personal endings** – the regular first‑aorist active endings:  
   - **‑α** (1 sg.)  
   - **‑ας** (2 sg.)  
   - **‑ε(ν)** (3 sg.)  
   - **‑αμεν** (1 pl.)  
   - **‑ατε** (2 pl.)  
   - **‑αν** (3 pl.)  

Putting the pieces together:  

```
ε + (stem‑σ) + personal ending
```

For **ἵστημι** → stem = ἱστη‑ → aorist stem = ἱστησ‑ → add augment ε‑ → ἐστησ‑ + endings.

---

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **First‑aorist formation** | Add **‑σ‑** to the present stem, then attach the augment and endings. | ἵστημι → ἐ‑στησ‑ + ‑α = ἔστησα |
| **Vowel contraction** | When the augment **ε‑** meets a vowel at the start of the stem, they may contract (e.g., **ε + ἀ** → **ἠ**). | ἀγοράζω → ἠγόρασα |
| **Reduplication for some verbs** | A few ‑μι verbs have a **second‑aorist** (no ‑σ‑) or an irregular aorist. | φῆμι → εἶπον (irregular) |
| **Irregular aorists** | Some very common ‑μι verbs do **not** follow the regular pattern. | ἔχω → ἔσχον; εἰμί → ἦν |
| **Spelling changes** | Before **‑σ‑**, a final **ν** of the stem may disappear (e.g., **τίθημι** → **ἔθηκα**). | τίθημι → ἔ‑θη‑κ‑α (aorist without ‑σ‑) – this is a **second‑aorist** form. |
| **Passive & Middle** | The aorist **passive** uses **‑θη‑** (first aorist) or **‑η‑** (second aorist); the **middle** uses **‑σ‑** plus middle endings (‑μην, ‑σο, ‑το). | ἵστημι → ἐστάθην (passive) |

### Quick checklist for regular ‑μι verbs  

1. Does the verb have a present stem ending in **‑μι**?  
2. Is the aorist **first‑aorist** (‑σ‑) or **second‑aorist** (‑κ‑/‑η‑)?  
3. If first‑aorist, add **ε‑** + **‑σ‑** + endings.  
4. If irregular, memorize the aorist form (e.g., φῆμι → εἶπον).

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (Aorist Indic. Active) | Transliteration | English |
|------------------------------|----------------|---------|
| **ἔστησα ἐπὶ τὸν λόφον.** | *éstēsa epì tòn lóphon.* | I stood on the hill. |
| **ἔστησαν οἱ στρατιῶται ἐν τῇ πόλει.** | *éstēsan hoi stratiôtai en tē̂i pólei.* | The soldiers stood in the city. |
| **ἔθηκας τὸ βιβλίον ἐπὶ τὸν πίνακα.** *(second‑aorist example)* | *éthēkas tò biblíon epì tòn pínaka.* | You placed the book on the board. |
| **εἶπον τὸ μυστήριον.** *(irregular aorist of φῆμι)* | *eîpon tò mystḗrion.* | I said the secret. |

## 5. Usage & Context  

* **Narrative past** – The aorist is the default past tense in stories, histories, and myths.  
* **Sequential actions** – When a series of completed actions is listed, each verb appears in the aorist.  
* **Single, punctual events** – Use the aorist for actions that happen once or are viewed as a whole.  
* **Contrast with imperfect** – The imperfect (continuous past) describes ongoing or repeated past actions; the aorist contrasts by presenting a “snapshot”.  

**Typical collocations**:  
- **ἔστησα** with location phrases (ἐπὶ, ἐν, ἐντὸς).  
- **ἔθηκα** with direct objects (accusative) and sometimes with **εἰς** + accusative for direction.  

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European roots** | The aorist suffix **‑σ‑** derives from the PIE *‑s‑* perfect‑aorist marker, used to indicate a completed action. |
| **Augment (ε‑)** | Originally a pre‑verb *e‑* meaning “past”. It appears in all Greek past tenses (aorist, imperfect, pluperfect). |
| **‑μι verbs** | The ending **‑μι** is the oldest Greek present‑active ending, cognate with Sanskrit **‑mi** (e.g., *bhárāmi* “I bear”). It survived only in a limited set of verbs. |
| **Shift to ‑σ‑ aorist** | Early Greek (Homeric) sometimes used a simple **‑α** (e.g., *ἔστη*), but by Classical Greek the **‑σ‑** aorist became the norm for regular ‑μι verbs. |
| **Irregularities** | Verbs like **φῆμι** and **εἰμί** preserve older, suppletive aorist forms (εἶπον, ἦν) that reflect different PIE roots. |

---

## 7. Summary  

* The **aorist indicative active** of **‑μι** verbs expresses a **single, completed past action** performed by the subject.  
* Regular formation: **augment ε‑ + aorist‑stem (‑σ‑) + personal endings** → e.g., **ἔστησα**.  
* Remember the **first‑aorist** pattern, but watch for **irregular aorists** (φῆμι → εἶπον, εἰμί → ἦν).  
* Use the aorist for narrative, punctual events, or when listing a sequence of actions.  

With this paradigm and the rules above, you can confidently recognize and produce the aorist indicative active of the most common ‑μι verbs in Ancient Greek. Happy reading and translating!
