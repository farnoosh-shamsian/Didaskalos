# Lesson: The Present Active (Indicative) and the Present Active Infinitive **‑ω / ‑εῖν**

---

## 1. What the “Present Active (‑ω)” Means  

In Ancient Greek the **present tense** expresses an action that is **currently occurring, habitual, or generally true**.  
When the verb is **active**, the subject **does** the action (as opposed to passive, where the subject receives it).

Two important forms of the present active are:

| Form | Function | Typical ending (1st sg.) |
|------|----------|--------------------------|
| **Present Active Indicative** | A finite verb that can stand as the main verb of a clause. | **‑ω** (e.g., *λύω* “I loosen”) |
| **Present Active Infinitive** | The “to‑verb” form, used after certain verbs, adjectives, or prepositions. | **‑εῖν** (e.g., *λύειν* “to loosen”) |

The **‑ω** you see in textbooks always refers to the **first‑person singular present active indicative**. The infinitive ends in **‑εῖν**, but it is built from the same present stem that gives the **‑ω** form.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1. The Present Active Stem  

1. **Regular (non‑contract) verbs** – take the **present stem** found in the first‑person singular present indicative **‑ω**.  
   *Example*: **λύ‑** (from *λύω* “I loosen”).

2. **Contract verbs** – the vowel of the stem contracts with the ending **‑ω**.  
   *Examples*:  
   - **τιμά‑** → *τιμῶ* (I honor)  
   - **φιλεῖ‑** → *φιλοῦ* (I love)  

### 2.2. Full Paradigm of a Regular Verb (λύω)  

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | **λύω** | **λύομεν** |
| 2nd | **λύεις** | **λύετε** |
| 3rd | **λύει** | **λύουσι(ν)** |

*Notes*  

- The **present active infinitive** is **λύειν**.  
- The **present active participle** (for reference) is **λύων, λύουσα, λῦον**.

### 2.3. Full Paradigm of a Contract Verb (τιμάω → τιμῶ)  

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | **τιμῶ** | **τιμῶμεν** |
| 2nd | **τιμᾷς** | **τιμᾶτε** |
| 3rd | **τιμᾷ** | **τιμῶσι(ν)** |

- **Infinitive**: **τιμᾶν** (note the contraction of *‑α + ‑ειν* → *‑ᾶν*).  
- The **contract** can be **‑α‑, ‑ε‑, ‑ο‑** depending on the vowel of the stem.

### 2.4. Quick Reference Table  

| Verb type | 1st sg. present active | Present infinitive |
|-----------|-----------------------|--------------------|
| Regular (λυ‑) | **λύω** | **λύειν** |
| Contract –α‑ (τιμα‑) | **τιμῶ** | **τιμᾶν** |
| Contract –ε‑ (φιλε‑) | **φιλοῦ** | **φιλεῖν** |
| Contract –ο‑ (προσαγο‑) | **προσαγοῦ** | **προσαγοῦν** |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Form the present stem** | Take the 1st‑sg. **‑ω** form, drop **‑ω** → present stem. | *λύω* → **λύ‑** |
| **2. Add endings** | Attach the appropriate present active endings (‑ω, ‑εις, ‑ει, ‑ομεν, ‑ετε, ‑ουσι(ν)). | *λύ‑* + ‑ει = **λύει** |
| **3. Contract verbs** | When the stem ends in a vowel, the vowel contracts with the ending. | *τιμα‑* + ‑ω → **τιμῶ** |
| **4. Infinitive formation** | Add **‑εῖν** to the present stem. For contract verbs, the vowel contracts to **‑ᾶν, ‑εῖν, ‑οῦν**. | *λύ‑* + ‑εῖν = **λύειν**; *τιμα‑* + ‑εῖν → **τιμᾶν** |
| **5. Spelling of the 3rd‑pl.** | The ending **‑ουσι(ν)** may appear as **‑ουσι** (without ν) after a vowel or diphthong. | *λύουσι* vs. *φιλοῦσι* |
| **6. Accent placement** | In the present active, the accent falls on the **penult** if it is long; otherwise on the **ultima**. | *λύω* (penult long) → accent on *λύ*; *τιμῶ* (penult short) → accent on *μῶ* |
| **7. Exceptions** | Some verbs have irregular stems (e.g., *εἰμί* “to be”, *ἔχω* “to have”). Their present forms must be memorised. | *εἰμί* → 1st sg. **εἰμί**, infinitive **εἶναι** |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (present active) | Transliteration | English |
|------------------------|----------------|---------|
| **Λύω τὸν δεσμόν.** | Lýō tòn desmón. | I loosen the knot. |
| **Τιμῶ τοὺς παῖδας.** | Timō toùs paîdas. | I honor the children. |
| **Φιλοῦμεν τὸν θεόν.** | Philoumen tòn theón. | We love God. |
| **Ἀκούειν τὸν λόγον.** | Akoúein tòn lógon. | To hear the word. |
| **Θέλειν εἰς τὴν πόλιν ἔρχεσθαι.** | Thélein eis tḕn pólīn érchesthai. | He wants to come into the city. |

*Note*: In the infinitive examples the **‑εῖν** (or contracted **‑ᾶν**) shows the “to‑verb” meaning.

---

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Narrative prose** – The present active is used for actions happening **now** or for **habitual** actions.  
2. **Philosophical or rhetorical discourse** – Present active often expresses **general truths** (*ὁ ἀνθρώπος φημὶ* “the man says”).  
3. **Infinitive** – Appears after:  
   * Verbs of **desire, intention, ability** (*βούλομαι, δύναμαι*).  
   * **Prepositions** that take the infinitive (*πρὸς, ἀπό*).  
   * **Adjectives** and **nouns** that require an infinitive complement (*ἕτοιμος εἶναι* “ready to be”).  

4. **Mood** – The present active infinitive is **non‑finite**; it cannot stand alone as the main verb of a clause without a governing word.

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** | The present active ending **‑ō** (Greek **‑ω**) goes back to the PIE *‑ō* (first‑person singular present). |
| **Old Greek** | Early Greek used a **‑ειν** infinitive for the present active, already present in Homeric Greek. |
| **Contract verbs** | The contraction of vowel + ending (e.g., *τιμα‑ + ω → τιμῶ*) reflects a phonological process that began in the **Mycenaean** period and became regular by the Classical era. |
| **Dialectal variation** | Some Ionic dialects showed **‑ειν** → **‑ναι** for the infinitive earlier, but the Classical standard settled on **‑εῖν** (with contracted forms **‑ᾶν, ‑εῖν, ‑οῦν**). |
| **Later Greek** | In Koine and Byzantine Greek the infinitive gradually fell out of use, being replaced by **periphrastic constructions** (e.g., *θέλω να* + subjunctive). |

---

### Take‑away  

- The **present active** tells us **who is doing what now**.  
- The **first‑person singular** always ends in **‑ω** (or a contracted form).  
- The **present active infinitive** ends in **‑εῖν** (or its contracted variants) and functions like English “to …”.  
- Master the **present stem** (found by dropping **‑ω**) and you can build all present active forms, regular and contract alike.
