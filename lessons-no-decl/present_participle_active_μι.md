# Lesson: The Present Active Participle (‑μι)  

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic noun declension, present indicative active endings*  

---

## 1. What is the Present Active Participle?

The **present active participle** (Greek **‑μι**) is a verbal adjective that expresses an **ongoing, simultaneous action** performed by the subject of the main verb.  

- **Aspect** – *present*: the action is happening at the same time as the main verb.  
- **Voice** – *active*: the subject is the doer of the action.  
- **Mood** – *participle*: it functions like an adjective (it can be declined) and can also be used substantively (as a noun).  

Typical English equivalents: “‑ing” forms (*running, seeing, loving*) or “who/which is …” (*the man who is speaking*).

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

The present active participle is formed from the **present stem** of a verb plus the participial suffix **‑μι**. The suffix is added to the **thematic vowel** (‑ο‑ for most verbs, ‑ε‑ for contract verbs) and then the case‑number endings of a first‑/second‑declension adjective are attached.

### 2.1. Basic Formation  

| Verb type | Present stem | Participle stem (before endings) |
|-----------|--------------|----------------------------------|
| **‑ω** (regular) | λύ‑ (from λύω “to loose”) | λυ‑ + ‑ο‑ → **λυ‑** |
| **‑εω** (contract) | τιμ‑ε‑ (from τιμέω “to honor”) | τιμ‑ + ‑ε‑ → **τιμ‑** |
| **‑αω** (contract) | φιλ‑α‑ (from φιλάω “to love”) | φιλ‑ + ‑α‑ → **φιλ‑** |
| **Irregular** | εἰμί “to be” → **ὤν** (suppletive) | — |

After the stem, the **standard adjective endings** are attached:

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Nominative Singular** | ‑ων | ‑ουσα | ‑ον |
| **Genitive Singular**   | ‑οντος | ‑ουσης | ‑οντος |
| **Dative Singular**     | ‑οντι | ‑ουσῃ | ‑οντι |
| **Accusative Singular** | ‑οντα | ‑ουσαν | ‑ον |
| **Vocative Singular**   | ‑ων | ‑ουσα | ‑ον |
| **Nominative Plural**   | ‑οντες | ‑ουσαι | ‑οντα |
| **Genitive Plural**     | ‑οντων | ‑ουσῶν | ‑οντων |
| **Dative Plural**       | ‑ουσι(ν) | ‑ουσῖν | ‑ουσι(ν) |
| **Accusative Plural**   | ‑οντας | ‑ουσας | ‑οντα |
| **Vocative Plural**     | ‑οντες | ‑ουσαι | ‑οντα |

> **Note:** The dative plural may appear as **‑ουσι** (older) or **‑ουσιν** (later).

> The **contract** vowel (‑ε‑ or ‑α‑) merges with the thematic vowel, giving a long vowel (‑ε‑ → ‑ε‑, ‑α‑ → ‑α‑) before the participial suffix.

### 2.4. Suppletive Forms  

The verb **εἰμί** (“to be”) has a special present active participle **ὤν, ὥσα, ὄν** (masc., fem., neut.) which follows the same adjective endings but is irregular in stem.

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **1. Agreement** | The participle must **agree in gender, number, and case** with the noun it modifies, just like any adjective. |
| **2. Simultaneity** | The present active participle denotes an action **occurring at the same time** as the main verb. It cannot express a prior or subsequent action (use aorist or future participles for those). |
| **3. Use with the Article** | When the participle functions as a **noun** (substantive), it is normally **preceded by the definite article**: ὁ λύων “the one who is loosing”. |
| **4. Position** | The participle can appear **before or after** the noun it qualifies. In poetry, it often precedes the noun for emphasis. |
| **5. Verbs with a‑stem (α‑verbs)** | Verbs whose present stem ends in **‑α‑** (e.g., **φιλέω → φιλ‑α‑**) form the participle with **‑α‑** + ‑μι → **φιλ‑** (no extra vowel). The pattern is the same as regular verbs. |
| **6. Contract verbs** | The vowel of the contract (‑ε‑ or ‑α‑) merges with the thematic vowel, producing a **long vowel** before ‑μι (e.g., τιμ‑ε‑ → **τιμ‑**). |
| **7. Irregular stems** | Some verbs have irregular present stems (e.g., **ἔρχομαι → ἐρχ‑**). The participle follows the same pattern: **ἐρχ‑** + ‑ων → **ἐρχων**. |
| **8. Double‑consonant stems** | If the stem ends in a double consonant (‑σσ‑, ‑θθ‑, etc.), the participle retains the double (e.g., **πίνω → πιν‑** → **πίνων**). |
| **9. No participle for some verbs** | A few verbs lack a present active participle (e.g., **εἰμί** has the special form **ὤν**; **ἔχω** has **ἔχων**). Generally, most verbs do have one. |
| **10. Use with verbs of perception** | The participle can be used with verbs like **ὁρῶ, ἀκούω** to express “while seeing/hearing …”: ὁρῶν τὸν ἄνθρωπον “seeing the man”. |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (with transliteration) | English translation |
|------------------------------|---------------------|
| **ὁ λύων τὸν δεσμὸν ἀποτρέπει.** <br> *ho lúōn tòn desmòn apotrêi.* | “The one who is loosing the bond unties it.” |
| **Ἡ γυνὴ τῇ ἀγορᾷ ὁδὸν εὑρίσκει.** <br> *hē gynē tē̂i agorâi hodòn heurískei.* | “The woman who is walking in the market finds the road.” |
| **Τὰ παιδία τὰ παιδίζοντα ἐν τῷ κήπῳ χαίρουσιν.** <br> *tà paidía tà paidízonta en tôi kḗpō̂i chaírousin.* | “The children who are playing in the garden are happy.” |
| **Ἐγὼ ἀκούων τὸν λόγον ἀποκρίνεσθαι οὐ δύναμαι.** <br> *egṓ akoúōn tòn lógon apokrinésthai ou dúnamai.* | “I, hearing the speech, am unable to answer.” |
| **Ὀρῶν τὸν ἥλιον ἀνατέλλειν, ἐξέλθωμεν.** <br> *orôn tòn hḗlion anatéllein, exélthōmen.* | “Seeing the sun rise, let us go out.” |

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Descriptive adjectives** – Often used to give a vivid, dynamic description: *ὁ τρέχων ἄνθρωπος* “the running man”.  
2. **Relative clauses** – The participle can replace a relative clause with *ὅς/ἥ/ὅ* when the action is simultaneous: *ὁ ἀνὴρ ἀγοράζων* = *ὁ ἀνὴρ ὅς ἀγοράζει* (“the man who is buying”).  
3. **Substantive (noun) use** – With the article, the participle can stand alone as a noun: *ὁ λύων* “the one who is loosing”.  
4. **With verbs of perception** – As shown above, the participle expresses “while …ing”.  
5. **Poetic economy** – In poetry, the participle can pack information that would otherwise require a full clause, allowing tighter meter.  

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

- The **‑μι** participial suffix is inherited from **Proto‑Indo‑European** *‑men* (the same source as the Latin **‑ens** in *amans* “loving”).  
- In **Mycenaean Greek**, the participle appears as **‑mi‑** attached to stems, already showing the same case endings as adjectives.  
- Over time, the participle became fully integrated into the **first‑/second‑declension adjective paradigm**, which is why its endings mirror those of regular adjectives.  
- The **suppletive form** **ὤν** for *εἰμί* reflects an older PIE participial stem *h₁es‑* (“to be”), preserved in many Indo‑European languages (e.g., Sanskrit *asti* “is”).
