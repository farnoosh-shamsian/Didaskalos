# Lesson: The Present Subjunctive Active (‑ω)  

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic noun declension, present indicative active endings.*

---

## 1. What is the Present Subjunctive Active?

The **subjunctive mood** expresses a *potential* or *non‑real* action: a wish, a possibility, a purpose, a condition, etc.  
The **present** tense places the action in the same time frame as the main verb (present, future, or general).  

The **active voice** means the subject of the verb performs the action (as opposed to the middle or passive).

In Ancient Greek the present active subjunctive is formed with the **ending ‑ω** (or its vowel‑length variants) attached to the verb stem. It is the “‑ω” form you will see in textbooks and in the original texts.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

Greek verbs fall into three main **thematic classes** (based on the vowel that appears before the personal endings in the present indicative):

| Class | Present indicative active stem | Typical ending in the present subjunctive |
|-------|------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| **‑ω** (contract‑ο) | ‑ο‑ (e.g., *λύ‑ω*) | ‑ω, ‑ῃς, ‑ῃ, ‑ωμεν, ‑ητε, ‑ωσι(ν) |
| **‑εω** (contract‑ε) | ‑ε‑ (e.g., *ποι‑ε‑ω*) | ‑ω, ‑ῃς, ‑ῃ, ‑ωμεν, ‑ητε, ‑ωσι(ν) |
| **‑αω** (contract‑α) | ‑α‑ (e.g., *φιλε‑α‑ω*) | ‑ῶ, ‑ᾷς, ‑ᾷ, ‑ῶμεν, ‑ᾶτε, ‑ῶσι(ν) |

### 2.1 Regular Paradigm (‑ω class) – Example: **λύ‑ω** “to loose, to release”

| Person | Subjunctive Active | Transliteration |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| 1st sg. | **λύω** | lýō |
| 2nd sg. | **λύῃς** | lýēs |
| 3rd sg. | **λύῃ** | lýē |
| 1st pl. | **λύωμεν** | lýōmen |
| 2nd pl. | **λύητε** | lýēte |
| 3rd pl. | **λύωσι(ν)** | lýōsi(n) |

> **Note:** The 3rd‑person plural may appear with or without the final **ν** (‑σι or ‑σιν). Both are correct; the **ν** is more common in prose.

### 2.2 ‑εω class – Example: **ποι‑ε‑ω** “to do, to make”

| Person | Subjunctive Active | Transliteration |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| 1st sg. | **ποιῶ** | poíō |
| 2nd sg. | **ποιῇς** | poíēs |
| 3rd sg. | **ποιῇ** | poíē |
| 1st pl. | **ποιῶμεν** | poíōmen |
| 2nd pl. | **ποιῆτε** | poíēte |
| 3rd pl. | **ποιῶσι(ν)** | poíōsi(n) |

### 2.3 ‑αω class – Example: **φιλε‑α‑ω** “to love, to be fond of”

| Person | Subjunctive Active | Transliteration |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|
| 1st sg. | **φιλῶ** | philô |
| 2nd sg. | **φιλεῖς** | phileîs |
| 3rd sg. | **φιλεῖ** | phileî |
| 1st pl. | **φιλῶμεν** | philômen |
| 2nd pl. | **φιλεῖτε** | phileîte |
| 3rd pl. | **φιλῶσι(ν)** | philôsi(n) |

### 2.4 Irregular Verbs  

| Verb | Present Subjunctive Active (1st sg.) | Remarks |
|------|--------------------------------------|---------|
| **εἰμί** “to be” | **ὦ** | Only the 1st sg. form is irregular; the rest follow the ‑ω pattern (εἶς, ἐστί, …). |
| **ἔχω** “to have, hold” | **ἔχω** (same as indicative) | The subjunctive is identical to the indicative because the stem already ends in ‑χ. |
| **ἴημι** “to go” | **ἴω** | Rare; most authors use the aorist subjunctive instead. |

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **No augment** | Unlike the past tenses (imperfect, aorist, perfect), the present subjunctive never takes the augment **ε‑**. |
| **Contracting** | When the stem ends in a vowel that contracts with the subjunctive ending, the vowel length changes: <br>‑ο + ‑ω → ‑ω (no change) <br>‑ε + ‑ω → ‑ω (e → o) <br>‑α + ‑ω → ‑ῶ (long α). |
| **Spelling of 2nd sg.** | In the ‑ω and ‑εω classes the ending is **‑ῃς** (with a long iota). In the ‑αω class it is **‑ᾷς**. |
| **3rd pl. ‑σι vs ‑σιν** | Both are acceptable; the **ν** is more common in later prose and in poetry for metrical reasons. |
| **Irregular 1st sg. of εἰμί** | The form **ὦ** is the only irregular present subjunctive active; all other persons are regular (εἶς, ἐστί, …). |
| **Middle‑voice forms** | The present subjunctive also exists in the middle (‑ωμαι, ‑ῃ, …) but this lesson covers only the active. |
| **Dialectal variation** | In some Doric texts the 2nd‑person singular may appear as **‑ῃ** (without the final sigma). This is rare in Attic. |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (with parsing) | Translation |
|----------------------|-------------|
| **Ἵνα λυθῇ τὸν δεσμὸν, ἔδειξον ἡμῖν τὴν ὁδὸν.** <br>*(ἵνα* + subjunctive of *λῦσις* “to be loosed”)* | “In order that the bond be loosed, show us the way.” |
| **Βούλομαι ἵνα ποιῶ τὸ ἔργον.** <br>*βούλομαι* + subjunctive of *ποιέω* | “I want to do the work.” |
| **Ἐλπίζω ὅπως ὁ φίλος μου φιλεῖ τὴν πόλιν.** <br>*ἐλπίζω* + subjunctive of *φιλέω* | “I hope that my friend loves the city.” |
| **Ἀνέσχομαι ἵνα ὦ ἐν τῇ ἀγορᾷ.** <br>*ἀνέσχομαι* + subjunctive of *εἰμί* | “I am willing to be in the marketplace.” |

*Notice the use of **ἵνα** (purpose/indirect command) and **ὅπως** (result/hope) which both require the present subjunctive when the action is contemporaneous with the main verb.*

---

## 5. Usage & Context  

| Context | Typical Trigger | Example |
|---------|----------------|---------|
| **Purpose or intention** (often after verbs of will, desire, command) | **ἵνα**, **ὅπως**, **ἵνα** + subjunctive | *βούλομαι ἵνα γράφω* “I want to write.” |
| **Indefinite or potential clauses** (after verbs of fearing, doubting, etc.) | **μή** + subjunctive (negative purpose) | *φοβοῦμαι μὴ λυθῇ* “I fear that it may be loosed.” |
| **Conditional clauses** (future more vivid) | **εἰ** + subjunctive (rare; usually optative) | *εἰ λυθῇ, χαίρω* “If it should be loosed, I rejoice.” |
| **Relative clauses of characteristic** (describing a typical action) | **ὅς, ἥ, ὅ** + subjunctive | *ὁ ἀνὴρ ὅς λυθῇ* “the man who (usually) looses …” |
| **After certain particles** | **ἄν** + subjunctive (potential) | *ἔσται ἂν λυθῇ* “it may be loosed.” |

The present subjunctive is **present** in time relative to the main verb, not necessarily “present” in absolute time. In a past main clause, the subjunctive still refers to the same time as that past verb (e.g., *ἔλεξα ὅτι λυθῇ* “I said that it would be loosed”).  

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

1. **Proto‑Indo‑European Roots** – The subjunctive originates from a *modal* suffix *‑y‑* attached to the verb stem, expressing potentiality. In Greek this suffix merged with the present tense vowel, giving the characteristic **‑ω** ending.  

2. **Early Greek** – In Mycenaean Greek the subjunctive is not directly attested, but the *‑y‑* element appears in later dialects. By the Classical period the **‑ω** form is fully grammaticalized as the present active subjunctive.  

3. **Vowel Contraction** – The three thematic classes reflect historical vowel contraction:  
   *‑ο‑* (original *‑o‑*), *‑ε‑* (original *‑e‑*), *‑α‑* (original *‑a‑*). The lengthening of *‑α* to *‑ῶ* in the subjunctive mirrors the same process that produced the long vowels of the present indicative (e.g., *φιλέω* → *φιλῶ*).  

4. **Irregular Forms** – The form **ὦ** for *εἰμί* preserves an older *‑ō* vowel that did not undergo the regular contraction pattern, showing the survival of a pre‑Greek subjunctive stem *\*es‑* > *εἰ‑* > *ὦ*.  

5. **Later Greek** – In Koine and Byzantine Greek the subjunctive survives but its distribution narrows; the optative takes over many of its functions. Nevertheless, the **‑ω** ending remains the hallmark of the present active subjunctive throughout the history of the language.
