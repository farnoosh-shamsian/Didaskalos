# Lesson: The Aorist Infinitive – Active Voice (ending ‑ω)

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic knowledge of verbs (person‑number endings, present infinitive ‑ειν).*

---

## 1. What is the Aorist Infinitive (Active)?

The **infinitive** is a non‑finite verb form that does not show person or number. In Ancient Greek it functions mainly as a noun‑like verb (subject, object, purpose, etc.).  

The **aorist** is a past‑time aspect that presents an action as a simple, completed whole, without reference to its internal duration.  

Putting the two together, the **aorist infinitive active** means “to do something once, as a completed act.” It is used:

* after verbs of saying, thinking, perceiving, etc. (e.g., *φημί* “I say” + aorist infinitive = “I say that …”)
* in purpose clauses (*ἵνα* + aorist infinitive)
* after certain prepositions (*πρὸ* + aorist infinitive = “before …”)

There are **two regular patterns**:

| Aorist type | Regular infinitive ending | Example (first aorist) | Example (second aorist) |
|-------------|---------------------------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| **First‑aorist** (σ‑stem) | **‑ειν** | λείψω → **λείψαι** “to leave” | — |
| **Second‑aorist** (no σ‑stem) | **‑ναι** | — | ἔλυσα → **λῦσαι** “to loose” |

In **early Greek** (Homeric, Ionic, and some later Attic), a **short infinitive ending ‑ω** appears for a few verbs, especially in poetry. It is a relic of an older infinitive form and is **rare** in Classical prose. The lesson will treat it as a **historical variant** of the aorist infinitive active.
