# Ancient Greek Alphabet – A Beginner’s Lesson

---

## 1. What Is the Alphabet?

The **Ancient Greek alphabet** is the set of 24 letters that were used to write Classical Greek (≈ 5th century BCE – 4th century CE).  
Each letter has a name, a common pronunciation, and a way to write it in the Latin alphabet (transliteration).

| #   | Uppercase | Lowercase | Name (Greek) | Transliteration | Typical Sound         |
| --- | --------- | --------- | ------------ | --------------- | --------------------- |
| 1   | Α         | α         | alpha        | a               | /a/ (short)           |
| 2   | Β         | β         | beta         | b               | /b/                   |
| 3   | Γ         | γ         | gamma        | g               | /ɣ/ (soft) /ɡ/ (hard) |
| 4   | Δ         | δ         | delta        | d               | /d/                   |
| 5   | Ε         | ε         | epsilon      | e               | /e/                   |
| 6   | Ζ         | ζ         | zeta         | z               | /z/                   |

## 2. Vowels and Diphthongs

### 2.1 Vowels

| Short               | Long                | Example (Attic)                               |
| ------------------- | ------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| α, ε, η, ι, ο, υ, ω | α, ε, η, ι, ο, υ, ω | **α** (αἰνός) – “good”; **η** (ἡμέρα) – “day” |

- **ε** and **ο** are always short.
- **η** and **ω** are always long.
- **α, ι, υ** can be short or long. When a long form is needed, a macron (ᾱ, ῑ, ῡ) is written.
- All vowels with a circumflex (e.g., ἕ) are long.

### 2.2 Diphthongs

A diphthong is a combination of two vowels pronounced as a single syllable. The second vowel is always **ι** or **υ**.

| Diphthong | Sound | Example                    |
| --------- | ----- | -------------------------- |
| αι        | /ai/  | **αἰ** (αἰνός) – “good”    |
| ει        | /ei/  | **εἰ** (εἰμί) – “I am”     |
| οι        | /oi/  | **οἰ** (οἰκία) – “house”   |
| αυ        | /au/  | **αὐ** (αὐτός) – “he”      |
| ευ        | /eu/  | **εὐ** (εὐτυχής) – “happy” |
| ου        | /ou/  | **οὐ** (οὐκ) – “not”       |
| ηυ        | /eu/  | **ηυ** (ηυγός) – “wet”     |
| υι        | /ui/  | **υι** (υἱός) – “son”      |

> **Special rule** – In the ᾳ, ῃ, ῳ forms the **ι** is written below the line (iota subscript) in older texts; in capital letters it is written on the line (adscript).

### 3.1 Assimilation & Doubling

- **Assimilation** – A consonant may change to match the following consonant (e.g., ἐ-πεμπ-θην → ἐ-πέμφ-θην).
- **Doubling** – Attic Greek often doubles consonants that are written as **σς** in Ionic (e.g., **ττ** for σς).
  - Example: **πρά̄ττω** (Attic) vs. **πρά̄σσω** (Ionic).
  - **ρρ** replaces **ρς** in later Attic (e.g., **θάρρος**).

## 4. Writing Rules & Exceptions

| Rule                      | Explanation                                                                              | Example                                        |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- |
| **Final sigma**           | Use **ς** only at the end of a word.                                                     | **ἄνθρωπος** → **ἄνθρωπος** (ends with **ς**). |
| **Long vs. short vowels** | Long vowels are marked with a macron or circumflex.                                      | **ῑ** (long i) in **ἔρριψεν**.                 |
| **Iota subscript**        | In diphthongs ᾳ, ῃ, ῳ the **ι** is written below the line in older texts.                | **ᾱ** in **Ἀθηναῖος**.                         |
| **Capital diphthongs**    | In capitals, the **ι** is written on the line (adscript).                                | **ΑΙ** in **ΑΙΘΗΝΑ**.                          |
| **Assimilation**          | A preceding consonant may assimilate to a following one.                                 | **ὀλ-νῡ-μι** (from ὄλλῡμι).                    |
| **Doubling**              | Attic doubles consonants that are σς in Ionic.                                           | **θάλαττα** (Attic) vs. **θάλασσα** (Ionic).   |
| **ρρ vs. ρς**             | Later Attic uses **ρρ** for **ρς** except in certain cases (dative plural, suffix ‑σις). |
