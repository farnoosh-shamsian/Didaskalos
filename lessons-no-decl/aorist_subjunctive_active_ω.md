# Lesson: The Aorist Subjunctive – Active Voice (‑ω)

**Target:** Beginner‑intermediate students of Ancient Greek  
**Focus:** How to form and use the **aorist subjunctive active** (ending in **‑ω**)  

---

## 1. What is the Aorist Subjunctive?

| Term | Meaning in Greek grammar | Typical English equivalent |
|------|--------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Aorist** | A past‑time aspect that presents an action as a simple, indivisible whole (a “single event”). | “did”, “wrote”, “went” |
| **Subjunctive** | A mood that expresses potential, purpose, command, or condition, often in subordinate clauses. | “may/would/should …”, “let …”, “in order that …” |
| **Active voice** | The subject performs the action. | “I wrote” (not “was written”) |

The **aorist subjunctive active** therefore expresses a **potential or intended single action** performed by the subject. In most contexts it appears in **purpose clauses**, **indefinite clauses**, or after certain verbs of fearing, wishing, commanding, etc.

The characteristic **ending** for the **first‑person singular** is **‑ω** (e.g., *γράψω* “may I write”). All other persons have their own endings, but they all contain the vowel **‑ω‑** in the stem.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1. Regular aorist active stem  

1. **Find the aorist active stem** (usually the 3rd‑person singular aorist active ending in **‑σεν**).  
   *Example*: **γράφ‑** → aorist active **γράψ‑** (from *ἔγραψεν* “he wrote”).  

2. **Add the subjunctive endings** (see table below).  

### 2.2. Subjunctive endings (active, aorist)

| Person | Ending | Example with *γράψ‑* (to write) |
|--------|--------|-----------------------------------|
| 1st sg | **‑ω** | **γράψω** – “may I write / I may write” |
| 2nd sg | **‑ῃς** (or **‑ῃς** after long vowel) | **γράψῃς** – “you may write” |
| 3rd sg | **‑ῃ** (or **‑ῃ**) | **γράψῃ** – “he/she/it may write” |
| 1st pl | **‑ωμεν** | **γράψωμεν** – “let us write” |
| 2nd pl | **‑ητε** (or **‑ητε**) | **γράψητε** – “you (pl.) may write” |
| 3rd pl | **‑ωσι(ν)** | **γράψωσι(ν)** – “they may write” |

> **Note:** The vowel of the ending may be long (‑ῃ‑) or short (‑ει‑) depending on the length of the preceding stem vowel. In most textbooks the long forms are shown because they are the regular outcome of the aorist subjunctive.

### 2.3. Full paradigm (regular verb *γράψω* “to write”)

|          | Singular | Plural |
|----------|----------|--------|
| **1st**  | **γράψω** | **γράψωμεν** |
| **2nd**  | **γράψῃς** | **γράψητε** |
| **3rd**  | **γράψῃ** | **γράψωσι(ν)** |

### 2.4. How to get the stem

| Verb type | Aorist active stem | Example |
|-----------|-------------------|---------|
| **-ω** (regular) | Add **‑σ‑** to the present stem, then replace **‑σ‑** with **‑ψ‑** (or **‑σ‑** if the verb is not a “‑ψ‑” verb) | *φιλέω* → *ἔφίλησα* (stem **φιλησ‑**) → subjunctive **φιλήσω** |
| **-μι** (e.g., *ἵστημι*) | Irregular; aorist stem often **‑στησ‑** | *ἵστημι* → *ἔστησα* → subjunctive **στήσω** |
| **Contract verbs** (e.g., *τιμάω*) | Usually **‑α‑** or **‑η‑** + **‑σ‑** → **‑ασ‑/‑ησ‑** → **‑ασω/‑ησω** | *τιμάω* → *ἐτίμησα* → subjunctive **τιμήσω** |
| **Irregular verbs** (e.g., *εἰμί*) | No aorist subjunctive active (the verb is defective). | — |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **A. Use the aorist active stem** | The subjunctive is built on the aorist active stem, not the present stem. | *γράφ‑* → *γράψ‑* → *γράψω* |
| **B. Length of the vowel before the ending** | If the stem ends in a **long vowel** (α, η, ω) the ending is **‑ῃ‑**; if it ends in a **short vowel** (ε, ι, ο, υ) the ending is **‑ῃ‑** (same spelling, but the quantity matters for meter). | *πράσσω* → stem **πράσ‑** (short ε) → *πράσῃ* |
| **C. Contract verbs** | For verbs whose present stem ends in a vowel + *α* or *η*, the aorist subjunctive often contracts to **‑ασω/‑ησω**. | *τιμάω* → *ἐτιμήσα* → *τιμήσω* |
| **D. Verbs with **‑μι** endings** | The aorist subjunctive active is formed from the aorist stem (often **‑σ‑**) plus the regular endings. | *ἵστημι* → *ἔστησα* → *στήσω* |
| **E. No aorist subjunctive for defective verbs** | Verbs like *εἰμί* (to be) lack an aorist active; they use other moods (optative, etc.) instead. | — |
| **F. The particle **ἵνα** + subjunctive** | The aorist subjunctive is the usual mood after *ἵνα* when the purpose is a single, completed action. | *ἔρχομαι ἵνα γράψω* – “I come so that I may write.” |
| **G. Negative particle** | Use **μή** (or **οὐ** in indirect questions) before the subjunctive. | *μή γράψῃς* – “let him not write.” |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (aorist subjunctive active) | Transliteration | English translation |
|-----------------------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| **ἵνα γράψω τὸν λόγον** | hína grápsō ton lógon | “so that I may write the word.” |
| **Φοβοῦμαι μὴ ἀποθάνῃ** | phoboûmai mē apothánē | “I fear that he may not die.” (purpose of avoiding death) |
| **Ἐλθὼν ἵνα ἀκούσω τὸν λόγον** | élthōn hína akoúsō ton lógon | “Having come, (so) that I may hear the speech.” |
| **Ἐλπίζω ὅπως ἀποδῶσιν τὰ δίκαια** | elpízō hópōs apodôsín tà díkaiα | “I hope that they may give back the just things.” |
| **Μὴ λυθῇ τὸ μυστήριον** (passive example for contrast) | Mē lythē tò mystḗrion | “Let the secret not be released.” |

*Only the first two sentences contain the **active** aorist subjunctive; the last two illustrate the mood in context.*

---

## 5. Notes on Usage & Context  

1. **Purpose clauses** – After *ἵνα* (so that) or *ὅπως* (in order that). The aorist subjunctive stresses a **single, completed** purpose.  
2. **Indefinite clauses** – After verbs of fearing, wishing, commanding, or after *μή* in prohibitions.  
3. **Conditional clauses** – In the **future more vivid** condition (protasis) the aorist subjunctive can appear, especially in poetry.  
4. **After *ὅπως* with negation** – Use *μή* + aorist subjunctive to express “lest”.  
5. **Poetic licence** – In epic and lyric poetry the aorist subjunctive may replace the present subjunctive for metrical reasons.

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** | The subjunctive mood derives from the PIE *‑y‑* (optative) and *‑s‑* (aorist) formations. The Greek aorist subjunctive combines the aorist *‑s‑* marker with the subjunctive *‑ω* ending, a development unique to Greek. |
| **Old Greek** | In Homeric Greek the aorist subjunctive is rare; the **optative** often serves the same function. By the Classical period the aorist subjunctive becomes the standard for purpose and indefinite clauses. |
| **Phonological change** | The original *‑s‑* (aorist) merged with the subjunctive vowel *‑o‑* → *‑ω*. The long vowel *‑ῃ‑* reflects compensatory lengthening after loss of *‑s‑* in some environments. |
| **Morphological analogy** | The endings of the aorist subjunctive are parallel to those of the present subjunctive (*‑ω*, *‑ῃς*, *‑ῃ*, etc.) but the stem is the aorist one, showing the “aspectual” nature of the mood. |
| **Later Greek** | In Koine the aorist subjunctive survives, though the distinction between aorist and perfect subjunctive becomes less sharp; the *‑ω* ending remains the hallmark of the active subjunctive. |

**Key take‑aways**

* Use the **aorist active stem** + subjunctive endings.  
* The mood expresses **potential, purpose, or indefinite** actions, usually a single, completed event.  
* Remember the particle **ἵνα** (or **ὅπως**) for purpose clauses, and **μή** for prohibitions.
