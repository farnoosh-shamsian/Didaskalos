# Lesson: The Aorist Active Participle (‑ων, ‑ουσα, ‑ον)

---

## 1. What the Aorist Active Participle Is  

| Term | Meaning in English | Greek Function |
|------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Aorist** | A “simple past” aspect – the action is viewed as a single, completed event. | The verb’s **aorist stem** (e.g., *λῡ‑* from *λῡ́ω* “to loose”). |
| **Active** | The subject of the participle performs the action. | The participle agrees with its **noun** in case, number, gender. |
| **Participle** | A verbal adjective; it can modify a noun or stand alone as a noun. | Used like an adjective, often in subordinate clauses (e.g., “when …”, “having …”). |

The **aorist active participle** therefore describes **“the one who *has* performed a completed action.”**  
It is formed from the **aorist stem** + the **suffix ‑σ‑** + the ordinary active‑participle endings.

> **Analogy:**  
> *λῡ́ω* (present) → aorist stem **λῡ‑** → λῡ‑σ‑ + endings → **λῡσ‑ων** “having loosed”.

---

## 2. How It Is Formed  

1. **Find the aorist stem** of the verb (the form that appears in the aorist indicative, e.g., *ἔλυσα* → *λῡ‑*).  
2. **Add the participial suffix ‑σ‑**.  
3. **Attach the active‑participle endings** (the same set used for the present active participle).  

### 2.1. The Active‑Participle Endings  

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Nominative** | ‑ων | ‑ουσα | ‑ον |
| **Genitive**   | ‑οντος | ‑ουσης | ‑οντος |
| **Dative**     | ‑οντι | ‑ουσῃ | ‑οντι |
| **Accusative** | ‑οντα | ‑ουσαν | ‑ον |
| **Vocative**   | ‑ων | ‑ουσα | ‑ον |

These endings are **identical** to those of the present active participle; the only difference is the **‑σ‑** before them.

## 3. Full Paradigm (Example with *λῡ́ω* “to loose”)  

Below the paradigm is shown for **singular, dual, and plural** in all five cases.  
The stem is **λῡσ‑** (aorist stem *λῡ‑* + ‑σ‑).

## 4. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example / Note |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **A. Use the aorist stem** | Do **not** use the present stem. | *γράφω* → aorist *ἔγραψα* → stem **γραφ‑** → *γραφ‑σ‑* → *γραφσ‑* → *γραφσ‑ων* (rare, see below). |
| **B. The ‑σ‑ suffix** | Always inserted **after** the aorist stem, before the endings. | *ἔλυσα* → *λῡ‑σ‑* → *λῡσ‑*. |
| **C. Contracted verbs** | If the aorist stem ends in a vowel, the ‑σ‑ may cause contraction. | *δείκνυμι* → aorist *ἔδειξα* (stem **δείξ‑**) → *δείξ‑σ‑* → *δείξων* (no extra vowel). |
| **D. Verbs with a **‑α‑** or **‑ε‑** aorist** | The aorist stem often ends in **‑α‑** or **‑ε‑**; the ‑σ‑ is added directly. | *πράττω* → aorist *ἔπραξα* (stem **πραξ‑**) → *πραξ‑σ‑* → *πραξ‑ων* → **πράξων**. |
| **E. Irregular stems** | Some verbs have a **different aorist stem** (e.g., *εἰμί* has no aorist; *εἶδον* → stem **εἰδ‑**). The participle is formed from that stem. | *εἶδον* → *εἰδ‑σ‑* → **εἰδών** “having seen”. |
| **F. Substantivized participles** | When used as nouns, the participle takes the normal noun declension (including the article). | **ὁ λύσων** “the one who has loosed”. |
| **G. No dual in Attic prose** | In most Attic prose the dual is rarely used; you will often see only singular and plural. | The dual forms above are shown for completeness. |
| **H. The aorist active participle is rarely used in the **future** sense** | It always denotes a **completed** action, never a future one. | Do not use *λῡσων* to mean “who will loose”. |

## 6. Usage & Context  

1. **Temporal clauses** – often introduced by *ὅτε*, *ἐπεί*, *ὡς* to mean “when, after, having”.  
   - *Ὅτε λύσων ἦν ὁ δεσμὸς, ἐξῆλθεν.* – “When the bond had been loosed, he went out.”  

2. **Causal or concessive participles** – can express cause or concession.  
   - *Λύσων τὸν δεσμὸν, ὁ ἥρως ἐπῆλθε τὴν πόλιν.* – “Having loosed the bond, the hero entered the city.”  

3. **Relative participles** – replace a relative clause.  
   - *Ὁ ἀνὴρ λύσων τὸν δεσμὸν ἦν ἀγαθός.* – “The man who had loosed the bond was good.”  

4. **Substantivized** – works as a noun, often with the article.  
   - *Ὁ λύσων ἦν ἡγεμών.* – “The one who had loosed (it) was the leader.”  

5. **Stylistic register** – more common in **literary, rhetorical, and historical** prose than in everyday conversation. In spoken Attic the aorist participle is relatively rare; the **perfect participle** or a **finite verb** often replaces it.
