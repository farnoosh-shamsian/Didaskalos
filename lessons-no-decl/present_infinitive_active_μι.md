# Lesson: The Present‑Active Infinitive in **‑μι**  

*Target: Beginner‑to‑intermediate students of Ancient Greek*  

---

## 1. What is the Present‑Active Infinitive **‑μι**?

In Ancient Greek the infinitive is a non‑finite verb form that can function like a noun (“to do …”).  
The **present‑active infinitive** expresses an action that is **simultaneous** with, or **continuous** relative to, the main verb.  

Most verbs form the present‑active infinitive with the suffix **‑ειν** (e.g., *λύειν* “to loosen”).  
A small, **archaic** class of verbs uses the older ending **‑μι**. These are the **‑μι‑verbs** and include the most common verb **εἰμί** “to be” and many **‑δωμι**‑type verbs (e.g., *δίδωμι* “to give”).  

> **Why it matters** – The **‑μι** infinitive is the form you will meet in classical texts, especially in poetry, drama, and early prose. Recognising it helps you read correctly and understand the subtle shades of meaning that the older form can convey.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1 The Basic Paradigm  

The present‑active infinitive **‑μι** has only one shape (no person, number, or gender).  
It is attached directly to the verb stem.

| Verb (dictionary form) | Present‑active infinitive **‑μι** |
|------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| εἰμί *to be*           | **εἶναι** (older **εἶν** → **εἶναι**) |
| δίδωμι *to give*       | **διδόναι** (older **διδόν** → **διδόναι**) |
| ἵστημι *to stand*      | **ἱστάναι** (older **ἱστάν** → **ἱστάναι**) |
| ἔρχομαι *to come* (deponent) – *no* **‑μι** infinitive (uses **ἔρχεσθαι**) |
| ποιέω *to do*          | **ποιεῖν** (regular **‑ειν**, not **‑μι**) |

> **Note** – The “old” **‑μι** form is often written with a short **‑ν** before **‑αι** (e.g., *εἶναι*). In the earliest stages the infinitive ended simply in **‑μι** (e.g., *εἰμι*), but by Classical Greek the **‑ν** had been inserted and the final **‑αι** added.

### 2.2 Deriving the **‑μι** Infinitive  

1. **Identify the present‑stem** (the form that appears before the personal endings in the present system).  
2. **Add the suffix** **‑ναι** (historically **‑μι** → **‑ναι**).  

| Present‑stem | Add **‑ναι** → Infinitive |
|--------------|--------------------------|
| εἰ‑ (from *εἰμί*) | εἰ‑ + ναι = **εἶναι** |
| διδ‑ (from *δίδωμι*) | διδ‑ + ναι = **διδόναι** |
| ἱστ‑ (from *ἵστημι*) | ἱστ‑ + ναι = **ἱστάναι** |

The vowel before **‑ναι** often lengthens (ε → ει, ο → ου, etc.) according to the regular vowel‑lengthening rules of Greek.

### 2‑3. Comparison with the Regular **‑ειν** Infinitive  

| Verb | Regular **‑ειν** infinitive | **‑μι** infinitive | Meaning difference |
|------|----------------------------|-------------------|--------------------|
| λύω “to loosen” | **λῦσαι** (aorist) / **λῦειν** (present) | — | — |
| εἰμί “to be” | — | **εἶναι** | Only **‑μι** verb; expresses state of being |
| δίδωμι “to give” | **διδόναι** (‑μι) vs. **διδόναι** (same) | Same | The **‑μι** class keeps the older shape, but the resulting infinitive looks like a regular **‑ειν** form after the **‑ν** has been inserted. |

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Only a limited set of verbs use **‑μι**.** | The class includes **εἰμί**, **διδόναι**, **ἱστάναι**, **γίγνεσθαι** (deponent), and a handful of others. Most verbs use **‑ειν**. | *εἶναι* “to be”, *διδόναι* “to give”. |
| **2. The infinitive always ends in **‑ναι**.** | The original **‑μι** has been replaced by **‑ναι** in Classical Greek. The **‑ν** is part of the suffix, not the stem. | *εἶναι*, *διδόναι*, *ἱστάναι*. |
| **3. Vowel lengthening before **‑ναι** follows regular patterns.** | Short vowels often become long (ε → ει, ο → ου, α → η) when the **‑ν** is added. | *διδ‑* → *διδόναι* (ο lengthened). |
| **4. No personal endings on the infinitive.** | The infinitive is non‑finite; it does not change for person, number, or gender. | *εἶναι* is the same whether the subject is “I” or “they”. |
| **5. The infinitive can be used with the particles **ἵνα**, **ὡς**, **ὅπως**, etc.** | These introduce purpose, result, or indirect statement clauses. | *ἵνα εἶναι* “in order to be”. |
| **6. Exception: Some **‑μι** verbs have a **regular** **‑ειν** infinitive for the *aorist* or *future* (e.g., *διδόναι* vs. *διδόν* (aorist) → *διδόναι* is present; aorist infinitive is *διδόν* + **‑αι** → *διδόναι* again).** | The aorist infinitive of **‑μι** verbs often looks identical to the present infinitive, but the meaning is aorist. | *εἶναι* (present) vs. *εἶναι* (aorist) – context decides. |
| **7. Deponent verbs do not have a **‑μι** infinitive.** | Deponent verbs have middle/passive forms only; their infinitive ends in **‑σθαι**. | *ἔρχεσθαι* “to come”. |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (with transliteration) | Translation | Note |
|------------------------------|------------|------|
| **Θέλω εἶναι δίκαιος.** (Thélō eînai díkaios.) | “I want to be just.” | Infinitive **εἶναι** as complement of *θέλω*. |
| **Διδάσκειν ἐστὶν ἀγαθόν· διδόναι δὲ ἀγαθὸν ἐστίν.** (Didáskein estìn agathón; didónai dè agathon estín.) | “Teaching is good; giving is also good.” | Two infinitives: regular **‑ειν** (*διδάσκειν*) and **‑μι** (*διδόναι*). |
| **Ἡ γυνὴ ἱστάναι ἐβούλετο.** (Hē gynē histánai ebóletο.) | “The woman wanted to stand.” | **ἱστάναι** from *ἵστημι*. |
| **Ἀκούειν ὅτι εἶναι ἀλήθεια.** (Akoúein hóti eînai alḗtheia.) | “He hears that it is the truth.” | Infinitive used in indirect statement after *ὅτι*. |

---

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Complement of verbs of desire, intention, or perception** – *θέλω εἶναι*, *προτιμῶ διδόναι*, *ὁρῶ ἱστάναι*.  
2. **Purpose clauses** introduced by **ἵνα**, **ὡς**, **ὅπως** – *ἦλθεν ἵνα εἶναι ἐγγύς* “He came in order to be near.”  
3. **Indirect statements** after *λέγω, φημί, ὁρῶ* etc. – *λέγει ὅτι εἶναι ἀληθές* “He says that it is true.”  
4. **Nominal use** – the infinitive can act as a noun, often with the article: *τὸ εἶναι* “the being, existence”.  
5. **Poetic and archaic style** – the **‑μι** infinitive is more common in early poetry (Homer, Hesiod) and in formal prose (Thucydides, Demosthenes).

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Stage | Form | Remarks |
|-------|------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European** | *‑mi* (infinitive suffix) | The same suffix appears in Sanskrit (*‑ti*), Latin (*‑re*), and Old Irish (*‑id*). |
| **Mycenaean Greek** | *‑mi* (written as **-mi**) | The infinitive was a simple **‑mi** attached directly to the stem (e.g., *e-mi* “to be”). |
| **Archaic Greek** | **‑ναι** (insertion of **ν**) | The nasal **ν** was inserted for phonological reasons, giving **‑ναι**. |
| **Classical Greek** | **‑ναι** (spelled **‑ναι**, pronounced /-nai/) | The older **‑μι** is no longer heard; the spelling reflects the historical development. |
| **Later Greek (Koine, Byzantine)** | **‑ναι** persists, but many **‑μι** verbs are replaced by regular **‑ειν** forms (e.g., *εἶναι* stays, but *διδόναι* may be replaced by *διδόν* + **‑αι**). | The **‑μι** class shrinks, leaving only a handful of high‑frequency verbs. |

**Etymology of the suffix** – The Proto‑Indo‑European infinitive suffix *‑mi* is related to the root *men‑* “to think, to intend”. In Greek it became a marker of the infinitive mood, later reinforced by the nasal **ν** (perhaps from a participial *‑ν*).

Remember: **‑μι** infinitives are rare but essential for reading classical Greek fluently. Recognise them, know the pattern **stem + ναι**, and you’ll be able to translate many important passages with confidence.
