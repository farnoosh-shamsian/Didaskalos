# Lesson: The Future Indicative Active (‑ω) in Ancient Greek  

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, present‑indicative active endings, basic noun declension.*

---

## 1. What the Future Indicative Active Is  

The **future indicative active** expresses an action that **will happen** (or is about to happen) from the speaker’s point of view.  
In Ancient Greek it is formed with a **future stem** plus the **active personal endings** **‑ω, ‑εις, ‑ει, ‑ομεν, ‑ετε, ‑ουσι(ν)**.  

| Mood | Tense | Voice | Typical ending (1st sg.) |
|------|-------|-------|--------------------------|
| Indicative | Future | Active | **‑ω** |

The future is part of the **indicative mood**, which states facts or predictions, and it belongs to the **active voice**, where the subject performs the action.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1. The Future Stem  

The future stem is **not always the same** as the present stem. There are three main ways to obtain it:

| Verb type | How to get the future stem |
|-----------|----------------------------|
| **-ω verbs** (present stem ends in ‑ω) | Add **‑σ‑** to the present stem, then drop the final ‑ω. Example: *λύ‑ω* → *λυ‑σ‑* → **λυσ‑** |
| **-μι verbs** (present stem ends in ‑μι) | Replace **‑μι** with **‑σ**. Example: *φημί* → **φασ‑** |
| **Irregular verbs** (e.g., *εἰμί, ἔρχομαι, δίδωμι*) | Use a special future stem supplied by the lexicon. |
| **Verbs with a contracted vowel** (e.g., *ποιέω*) | The future stem often shows **‑σ‑** after the contracted vowel: *ποιέ‑ω* → **ποιήσ‑** |

> **Rule of thumb:** Most verbs form the future stem by inserting **‑σ‑** before the personal endings. Memorise the few irregular stems.

### 2.2. Full Paradigm (Active Voice, Indicative Mood, Future Tense)

Below is the complete set of endings attached to the future stem.

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | **‑ω** | **‑ομεν** |
| 2nd | **‑εις** | **‑ετε** |
| 3rd | **‑ει** | **‑ουσι(ν)** |

#### Example: *λύω* “to loose, to release”

| Person | Form | Literal gloss |
|--------|------|---------------|
| 1st sg. | **λυσω** | I will loose |
| 2nd sg. | **λυσεις** | you will loose |
| 3rd sg. | **λυσει** | he/she/it will loose |
| 1st pl. | **λυσομεν** | we will loose |
| 2nd pl. | **λυσετε** | you (pl.) will loose |
| 3rd pl. | **λυσουσι(ν)** | they will loose |

#### Example: *φημί* “to say”

| Person | Form | Gloss |
|--------|------|-------|
| 1st sg. | **φασω** | I will say |
| 2nd sg. | **φασεις** | you will say |
| 3rd sg. | **φασει** | he/she/it will say |
| 1st pl. | **φασομεν** | we will say |
| 2nd pl. | **φασετε** | you (pl.) will say |
| 3rd pl. | **φασουσι(ν)** | they will say |

#### Example: Irregular *εἰμί* “to be”

| Person | Form | Gloss |
|--------|------|-------|
| 1st sg. | **ἔσομαι** | I will be |
| 2nd sg. | **ἔσῃ** (or **ἔσει**) | you will be |
| 3rd sg. | **ἔσται** | he/she/it will be |
| 1st pl. | **ἐσόμεθα** | we will be |
| 2nd pl. | **ἔσεσθε** | you (pl.) will be |
| 3rd pl. | **ἔσονται** | they will be |

> **Note:** *εἰμί* uses the special future forms **ἔσομαι, ἔσται, ἔσονται**, etc., which do **not** follow the regular ‑σ‑ pattern.

### 2.3. Contracted Futures  

When the present stem ends in a contracted vowel (‑αω, ‑εω, ‑οω), the future stem often shows a **‑σ‑** after the contraction, and the endings may contract further.

| Present stem | Future stem | Example (verb) | Future 1st sg. |
|--------------|------------|----------------|----------------|
| ‑αω (e.g., *ποιέω*) | ‑ασ‑ | *ποιέω* → **ποιήσ‑** | **ποιήσω** |
| ‑εω (e.g., *δεῖ* – rare) | ‑εσ‑ | *δεῖ* → **δεσ‑** | **δεσομαι** (archaic) |
| ‑οω (e.g., *βοῶ*) | ‑οσ‑ | *βοῶ* → **βοσ‑** | **βοσω** (poetic) |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Insert ‑σ‑** | For regular verbs, add **‑σ‑** to the present stem before the endings. | *λύ‑ω* → *λυ‑σ‑* → *λυσω* |
| **Drop the final ‑ω** | When the present stem ends in **‑ω**, remove it before adding **‑σ‑**. | *παιδεύ‑ω* → *παιδευ‑σ‑* → *παιδευσω* |
| **‑μι → ‑σ** | Verbs ending in **‑μι** replace **‑μι** with **‑σ**. | *φη‑μι* → *φασ‑* → *φασω* |
| **Irregular stems** | Some verbs have unique future stems (e.g., *εἰμί, ἔρχομαι, δίδωμι*). | *εἰμί* → *ἔσομαι* |
| **Contracted futures** | Contractions may cause vowel lengthening or loss of a vowel before **‑σ‑**. | *ποιέ‑ω* → *ποιήσ‑* → *ποιήσω* |
| **Spelling of 3rd‑pl. ending** | The ending is **‑ουσι** (or **‑ουσιν**). In poetry the **ν** may be omitted. | *λυσουσι(ν)* |
| **Middle‑voice future** | Not covered here, but note that the middle uses **‑σομαι, ‑σῃ, ‑σεται, ‑σόμεθα, ‑σεσθε, ‑σονται**. | — |
| **Future of “to be”** | Uses **ἔσομαι, ἔσται, ἔσονται** etc., not the regular pattern. | — |

## 5. Notes on Usage & Context  

1. **Predictive vs. Intentional** – The future can express a simple prediction (*ἔσται καλὸν* “it will be beautiful”) or a speaker’s intention (*ποιήσω* “I will do it”).  
2. **Future of Intent** – Often used with verbs of saying, thinking, or promising (*ὁ ἄνθρωπος ἐρῶν ὅτι φάσομαι* “the man says that he will speak”).  
3. **Literary Style** – In epic poetry the future may appear with the particle **ἄν** to indicate potential future action (*ἔσται ἂν* “it might become”).  
4. **Temporal Clauses** – Future indicative is frequently used in subordinate clauses introduced by **ὅταν** (when) or **ἐὰν** (if) when the condition is expected to be fulfilled.  
5. **Future Perfect** – Ancient Greek also has a **future perfect** (e.g., *λελύσομαι* “I will have loosed”), but that is a separate formation and not covered here.

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** | The future stem with **‑s‑** reflects the PIE *‑s‑* suffix used for prospective aspect. Many Indo‑European languages retain a similar marker (e.g., Latin *‑bō* is a later development, but the *‑s‑* appears in the Old Latin future of *ferre* → *feram*). |
| **Greek evolution** | Early Greek (Mycenaean) shows the *‑s‑* future in the form *-s-*, which later became the regular future marker in Classical Greek. The *‑σ‑* is a direct continuation of the PIE *‑s‑* suffix. |
| **Irregular verbs** | Verbs like *εἰμί* preserve an older, suppletive future stem (*εσ‑*), showing that the verb “to be” had a distinct paradigm already in Proto‑Greek. |
| **Phonological changes** | The insertion of *‑σ‑* sometimes caused contraction or lengthening of the preceding vowel (e.g., *ποιέω* → *ποιήσ‑*). This reflects the Greek tendency to avoid hiatus and to preserve syllable weight. |
| **Later Greek** | In Koine and Modern Greek the future is formed with the particle **θα** + present indicative, a development that replaced the classical *‑σ‑* formation. The classical future therefore provides a window onto an older stage of the language. |
