# Lesson: The Aorist Optative Active (ending ‑ω)  

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic verb stems, present‑active‑indicative endings.*

---

## 1. What is the Aorist Optative Active?

| Category | Meaning in English | Typical English equivalents |
|----------|-------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Mood** | **Optative** – expresses a wish, a potential action, a polite request, or a “may it be that …” feeling. | “may I …”, “let him …”, “I wish that …” |
| **Aspect** | **Aorist** – a simple, punctual action, not viewed as ongoing. | “to do (once)”, “to have done” |
| **Voice** | **Active** – the subject performs the action. | – |

The aorist optative is therefore the “may‑have‑done” form: *may I have done it*, *let him do it*, *I wish I had done it*, etc.

In Classical Greek the aorist optative is used:

* after verbs of **fear, doubt, or uncertainty** (e.g., φοβέομαι — *I fear that…*),  
* in **indirect questions** introduced by *εἰ* (if‑clauses),  
* in **polite requests** or **wishes** (especially in poetry),  
* in **conditional sentences** of the “future less vivid” type (rare).

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1. The Regular (non‑contract) Paradigm  

For a regular verb the aorist stem is the **aorist‑active‑stem** (usually the 3rd‑person‑aorist‑indicative form without *‑ε*).  
Add the **optative endings** shown in the table.

| Person | Ending | Example with *λύω* (to loosen) → aorist stem **λῡ‑** |
|--------|--------|------------------------------------------------------|
| 1 sg   | ‑οιμι   | λῡοιμι  “may I loosen” |
| 2 sg   | ‑οις    | λῡοις   “may you loosen” |
| 3 sg   | ‑οι     | λῡοι    “may he/she/it loosen” |
| 1 pl   | ‑οιμεν  | λῡοιμεν “may we loosen” |
| 2 pl   | ‑οιτε   | λῡοιτε  “may you (pl.) loosen” |
| 3 pl   | ‑οιεν   | λῡοιεν  “may they loosen” |

*Note:* In Attic prose the 3 sg often appears as **‑οι**; in poetry the **‑οι** may be shortened to **‑οι** (no change).  

---

### 2.2. The Contract‑Verb Paradigm (ending ‑ω)  

Many frequently used verbs are **contract verbs**: their stems end in **‑α** or **‑ε** (or a diphthong) and contract with the optative endings.  
The resulting 1 sg ending is **‑ῶ** (omega with circumflex), which is the “‑ω” the lesson title refers to.

| Person | Ending (contract) | Example with **διδῶ** (from *δίδωμι* “to give”) |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| 1 sg   | **‑ῶ**            | δῶ   “may I give” |
| 2 sg   | **‑ῇς**           | δῇς  “may you give” |
| 3 sg   | **‑ῇ**            | δῇ   “may he/she/it give” |
| 1 pl   | **‑ῶμεν**         | δῶμεν “may we give” |
| 2 pl   | **‑ῆτε**          | δῆτε  “may you (pl.) give” |
| 3 pl   | **‑ῶσι(ν)**       | δῶσι(ν) “may they give” |

**Why the ending looks like “‑ω”?**  
The underlying optative ending is **‑οι** (as in the regular paradigm). With a contract stem ending in **‑α/‑ε**, the sequence **‑α‑οι** contracts to **‑ῶ** (long omega). The same contraction produces the other forms (‑ῇς, ‑ῇ, …).

#### Common contract verbs (aorist optative active)

| Verb (inf.) | Aorist stem | 1 sg aorist opt. active |
|-------------|-------------|--------------------------|
| **διδῶ** (from *δίδωμι*) | διδ‑ | δῶ |
| **εἰδῶ** (from *εἰδέναι*) | εἰδ‑ | εἰδῶ |
| **ἔλθω** (from *ἦλθον*) | ἐλθ‑ | ἔλθω |
| **πράξω** (from *πράξα*) | πρ‑ | πρῶ (rare, poetic) |
| **γέγωνα** (from *γίγνομαι*) – irregular, see § 3.2 |

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **3.1. Form the aorist‑active stem** | For regular verbs, take the 3rd‑person‑aorist‑indicative without the final *‑ε* (e.g., *ἔλυσα* → *λῡ‑*). For contract verbs, the aorist stem already ends in *‑α/‑ε* (e.g., *διδ‑*). |
| **3.2. Add the optative endings** | Use the regular endings (‑οιμι, ‑οις, …) for non‑contract verbs; use the contracted set (‑ῶ, ‑ῇς, …) for contract verbs. |
| **3.3. Contracting rule** | When a stem ending in *‑α* or *‑ε* meets *‑οι*, the sequence contracts to *‑ῶ* (1 sg) and similarly for the other persons (‑ῇς, ‑ῇ, ‑ῶμεν, ‑ῆτε, ‑ῶσι(ν)). |
| **3.4. Spelling of the circumflex** | The circumflex on *‑ῶ* (and *‑ῇ*, *‑ῆ*) indicates a long vowel that bears the accent on the same syllable as the ending. |
| **3.5. Irregular aorist stems** | Verbs like *εἰμί* (to be) have a special aorist optative: **εἴην**, **εἴης**, **εἴη**, **εἴημεν**, **εἴητε**, **εἴησαν** (no ‑ω). |
| **3.6. The “‑οι” vs. “‑οιεν”** | In poetry the 3 pl ending **‑οι** may appear without the final *‑εν* (e.g., *δῶ* instead of *δῶσι*). |
| **3.7. Use of the aorist optative in prose** | Rare in Classical prose; more common in poetry, especially in Homeric and later lyric. When it appears, it often conveys a wish or a potential action. |
