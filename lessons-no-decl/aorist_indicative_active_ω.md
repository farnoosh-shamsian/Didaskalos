# Lesson: The Aorist Indicative Active ( ‑σ‑ forms)

---

## 1. What the Aorist Indicative Active Is  

| Term | Meaning in Greek grammar | Rough English equivalent |
|------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Aorist** | A “simple” past tense that presents an action as a whole, without reference to its duration, repetition, or completion. | “He/She/It **did** …” |
| **Indicative** | The mood of ordinary statements of fact (as opposed to subjunctive, optative, etc.). | “He **did** …” |
| **Active** | The subject performs the action (as opposed to middle or passive). | “He **did** …” |

The **aorist indicative active** therefore tells us that **the subject performed a completed action** in the past. It is the most common way to express a simple past event in Classical Greek.

> **Important:** The aorist active does **not** end in **‑ω**. The ending **‑ω** belongs to the **present** active indicative (e.g., λύω “I loosen”). The aorist active uses the **‑σ‑** (or **‑ε‑**) augment and a set of personal endings (‑α, ‑ας, ‑ε(ν), ‑αμεν, ‑ατε, ‑σαν). This lesson will focus on those true aorist forms.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1. Regular (First‑Conjugation) Aorist Active  

Most **-ω** verbs form a **regular (or “first”) aorist** by adding the **augment** (ε‑) to the stem, inserting **‑σ‑**, and then attaching the personal endings.

**Template**

```
ε‑ + (stem) + σ + (personal ending)
```

| Person | Ending | Example with λύω “to loosen” |
|--------|--------|------------------------------|
| 1st sg. | ‑α | ἔλυσα  “I loosened” |
| 2nd sg. | ‑ας | ἔλυσας “you (sg.) loosened” |
| 3rd sg. | ‑ε (or ‑ε(ν) before a vowel) | ἔλυσε(ν) “he/she/it loosened” |
| 1st pl. | ‑αμεν | ἐλύσαμεν “we loosened” |
| 2nd pl. | ‑ατε | ἐλύσατε “you (pl.) loosened” |
| 3rd pl. | ‑σαν | ἔλυσαν “they loosened” |

> **Note:** The augment **ε‑** is added only to the **first person singular** and to **all other forms** when the stem begins with a consonant. If the stem already begins with a vowel, the augment is a lengthening of that vowel (e.g., *ἦλθον* from *ἔρχομαι*).

### 2.2. Regular (Second‑Conjugation) Aorist Active  

Verbs whose stems end in a **vowel** (often **‑ε‑** or **‑ο‑**) form a **second aorist** without the **‑σ‑**. The augment is still added.

**Template**

```
ε‑ + (vowel‑stem) + (personal ending)
```

| Person | Ending | Example with ἀκούω “to hear” (stem ἀκου‑) |
|--------|--------|-------------------------------------------|
| 1st sg. | ‑α | ἤκουσα “I heard” |
| 2nd sg. | ‑ας | ἤκουσας |
| 3rd sg. | ‑ε(ν) | ἤκουσε(ν) |
| 1st pl. | ‑αμεν | ἀκουσαμεν |
| 2nd pl. | ‑ατε | ἀκουσατε |
| 3rd pl. | ‑σαν | ἤκουσαν |

### 2.3. Full Paradigm (First‑Conjugation)  

Below is the complete active aorist indicative paradigm for a regular **‑ω** verb (λύω) and a regular **‑ε‑** verb (ἀκούω) side‑by‑side.

| Person | λύω (first aorist) | ἀκούω (second aorist) |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------------|
| **1st sg.** | ἔλυσα | ἤκουσα |
| **2nd sg.** | ἔλυσας | ἤκουσας |
| **3rd sg.** | ἔλυσε(ν) | ἤκουσε(ν) |
| **1st pl.** | ἐλύσαμεν | ἀκουσαμεν |
| **2nd pl.** | ἐλύσατε | ἀκουσατε |
| **3rd pl.** | ἔλυσαν | ἤκουσαν |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Augment (ε‑) is required** | Adds past‑time sense. If the stem begins with a vowel, the augment lengthens that vowel (e.g., *ἦλθον* from *ἔρχομαι*). | *λύω → ἔλυ‑* |
| **‑σ‑ appears only in first‑conjugation aorists** | Verbs whose stems end in a consonant use ‑σ‑; those ending in a vowel do not. | *λύω → ἔλυ‑σ‑α* vs. *ἀκούω → ἤκου‑α* |
| **Contract verbs** | Verbs whose present stem ends in a contracted vowel (e.g., *φιλέω* → *φίλησα*) drop the contract and add ‑σ‑ as usual. | *φιλέω → ἐφίλησα* |
| **Irregular aorists** | Some high‑frequency verbs have unique aorist stems (e.g., *εἰμί → ἦν*, *ἔρχομαι → ἦλθον*, *λαμβάνω → ἔλαβον*). These must be memorised. | *εἰμί → ἦν* |
| **Spurious diphthongs** | In some dialects, the augment may appear as a diphthong (e.g., *ἔ* → *ἠ*). In Attic Greek the simple ε‑ is standard. | – |
| **Middle‑passive forms** | The aorist active is distinct from the aorist middle/passive (which use ‑θην, ‑θη, ‑θημεν, etc.). Do not confuse the endings. | *λύω* active aorist: ἔλυσα; middle/passive aorist: ἐλύθην |

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Narrative Past** – The aorist active is the default tense for recounting events in histories, myths, and dramas.  
2. **Sequential Actions** – When a series of actions is listed, each verb often appears in the aorist active (e.g., *ἦλθον, εἶδον, ἐνίκησα* “I came, I saw, I conquered”).  
3. **Perfect‑like Result** – Although the aorist does not express the result itself (that is the perfect), it can be used when the result is obvious from context.  
4. **Conditional Clauses** – In past unreal conditions, the aorist indicative appears in the protasis (if‑clause) with *εἰ* (e.g., *εἰ ἔλυσα τὸν δεσμὸν, ἔσται ἐλευθέριος* “If I untied the bond, he will be free”).  

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

- **Proto‑Indo‑European Roots** – The aorist suffix **‑σ‑** derives from the PIE *‑s‑* perfective marker, which in Greek became a dedicated past‑tense marker.  
- **Augment Origin** – The augment **ε‑** likely began as a pre‑verbally placed *e* particle indicating past time; it later fused to the verb stem.  
- **Dialectal Variation** – In early Ionic and Homeric Greek, the augment sometimes appears as a lengthened vowel (*ἦλθον* from *ἔρχομαι*). By Classical Attic, the simple ε‑ is standard.  
- **Middle‑Passives** – The aorist active co‑existed with aorist middle/passive forms, reflecting the three‑voice system that developed in Greek from earlier PIE active‑middle distinctions.
