# Lesson: Introduction to Greek Adjectives

## 1. What an Adjective Does

An **adjective** describes or qualifies a noun. In Ancient Greek, adjectives can express ideas such as:

- size: **small**, **great**
- quality: **good**, **beautiful**, **just**
- quantity: **many**, **few**
- relation or identity: **human**, **divine**, **Greek**

Like nouns, Greek adjectives are **inflected**. That means their forms change depending on their grammatical role in the sentence.

A Greek adjective usually tells you three things:

- **gender**
- **number**
- **case**

Because of this, adjectives are closely tied to the nouns they describe.

---

## 2. The Core Rule: Agreement

The most important principle is:

> **An adjective agrees with its noun in gender, number, and case.**

This does **not** mean the adjective always has the exact same ending as the noun. It means the adjective must match the noun grammatically.

### Agreement categories

#### Gender

An adjective must match the noun as:

- **masculine**
- **feminine**
- **neuter**

#### Number

An adjective must match the noun as:

- **singular**
- **plural**
- sometimes **dual** (rare)

#### Case

An adjective must match the noun’s case:

- **nominative**
- **genitive**
- **dative**
- **accusative**
- **vocative**

### Example idea

If the noun is:

- masculine
- singular
- nominative

then the adjective describing it must also be:

- masculine
- singular
- nominative

So if a noun means “the man,” and you want to say “the good man,” the adjective “good” must appear in the masculine singular nominative form.

---

## 3. Why Agreement Matters

Ancient Greek has a more flexible word order than English. Since endings carry grammatical information, Greek does not rely as heavily on position alone.

That means an adjective can often appear:

- right next to its noun
- separated from its noun
- before it
- after it

Even when separated, the adjective can still clearly belong to the noun because the forms match in **gender, number, and case**.

This is one reason agreement is so important: it helps the reader connect words across the sentence.

---

## 4. General Structure of Adjectives

Greek adjectives are usually learned through their **gendered forms**, often given in dictionary style as:

- masculine form
- feminine form
- neuter form

For example, an adjective might be listed as:

- masculine ending
- feminine ending
- neuter ending

This tells you how the adjective declines.

Many adjectives follow patterns similar to noun declensions.

---

## 5. Main Adjective Patterns

## A. First and Second Declension Adjectives

These are the most common and often the easiest to learn first.

They typically use:

- **second declension endings** for masculine and neuter
- **first declension endings** for feminine

A very common pattern is:

- masculine: like a second-declension masculine noun
- feminine: like a first-declension feminine noun
- neuter: like a second-declension neuter noun

This is the pattern of many common adjectives such as “good,” “just,” “beautiful,” and so on.

### What that means in practice

A single adjective has three related sets of forms:

- one for masculine nouns
- one for feminine nouns
- one for neuter nouns

The stem usually stays recognizable, while the ending changes according to gender, number, and case.

---

## B. Third Declension Adjectives

Some adjectives follow **third declension** patterns, either partly or fully.

These can be more complex because:

- the endings are less uniform
- the stem may vary
- masculine/feminine may share one form, while neuter has another

These adjectives still obey the same agreement rule:
they must match the noun in **gender, number, and case**.

So even when the declension is harder, the function is the same.

---

## 6. Adjectives and Nouns: How They Work Together

Adjectives can be used in two major ways:

### 1. Attributive use

The adjective directly describes a noun:

- “the good man”
- “a wise woman”
- “beautiful gifts”

In this use, the adjective is attached to a noun as one of its modifiers.

### 2. Predicate use

The adjective functions more like part of the statement:

- “the man is good”
- “the gifts are beautiful”

In this use, the adjective is not merely attached inside the noun phrase; it says something about the subject.

This distinction matters in Greek, because the **article** often helps show whether the adjective is attributive or predicate.

---

## 7. Attributive Position

An adjective is **attributive** when it belongs directly to a noun phrase and modifies the noun.

Greek often marks this with the article.

### Common attributive patterns

#### Pattern 1: Article + Adjective + Noun

- “the good man”

#### Pattern 2: Article + Noun + Article + Adjective

- literally something like “the man, the good one”

Both patterns are normal Greek ways to show that the adjective belongs closely to the noun.

### Key idea

When the adjective is attributive, it is usually enclosed in the noun phrase by article patterns that clearly connect it to the noun.

---

## 8. Predicate Position

An adjective is **predicate** when it states something about the noun rather than sitting inside the noun phrase.

Examples in English:

- “the man is good”
- “the law is just”

In Greek, predicate adjectives often appear **without the article** in relation to an articular noun.

### Common predicate patterns

#### Article + Noun + Adjective

This often means:

- “the man is good”

#### Adjective + Article + Noun

This can also be predicate:

- “good is the man” or “the man is good,” depending on context

### Key idea

If the noun has the article but the adjective does not enter the article structure, the adjective is often predicate rather than attributive.

---

## 9. Substantive Use of Adjectives

Sometimes an adjective appears **without an expressed noun** and functions almost like a noun itself.

This is called **substantive** use.

Examples of the idea:

- “the good”
- “the wise”
- “the dead”
- “the poor”

Greek does this very naturally, especially when the article is present.

So an adjective can:

- modify a noun
- make a statement about a noun
- stand in for a noun

---

## 10. Word Order: Where Adjectives Show Up

Greek word order is flexible, but not random. Adjectives can appear in several places depending on emphasis, style, and syntax.

### A. Before the noun

An adjective may come before the noun.

This is common, especially in straightforward descriptive phrases.

### B. After the noun

An adjective may come after the noun.

This is also common.

### C. Separated from the noun

An adjective may be separated from its noun by other words.

Because agreement is visible in the endings, the reader can still identify which noun the adjective modifies.

### D. Repeated article structures

Greek often uses article repetition to clarify the relationship:

- article + noun + article + adjective

This can sound unusual in English, but it is a normal Greek structure.

---

## 11. Does Word Order Change the Meaning?

Sometimes yes, but not always.

### What usually stays the same

The basic descriptive relationship is usually determined by:

- agreement
- article usage
- overall syntax

### What word order can affect

Word order may affect:

- emphasis
- focus
- style
- contrast

For example, placing the adjective first can give it extra prominence. Delaying the adjective may create suspense or rhetorical weight.

So Greek word order is not just grammatical; it can also be expressive.

---

## 12. How to Identify What an Adjective Is Doing

When you see an adjective in a sentence, ask these questions:

### 1. Which noun does it agree with?

Check:

- gender
- number
- case

This is your first clue.

### 2. Is it attributive or predicate?

Look at the article structure.

- If the adjective is inside an article pattern with the noun, it is probably **attributive**.
- If the adjective is outside that structure, it may be **predicate**.

### 3. Is there even a noun expressed?

If not, the adjective may be **substantive**.

### 4. Is the adjective in a familiar declension pattern?

That helps you recognize the form and connect it to the right noun.

---

## 13. Comparison of Adjectives

Like English adjectives, Greek adjectives can express degrees:

- **positive**: good
- **comparative**: better / more good
- **superlative**: best

These forms are built differently depending on the adjective, but the general idea is the same:
Greek adjectives can express increasing degree or intensity.

These comparative and superlative forms still behave like adjectives and still agree with the nouns they describe.
