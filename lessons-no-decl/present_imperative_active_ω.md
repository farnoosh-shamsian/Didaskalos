# Lesson: The Present Active Imperative (‑ω) in Ancient Greek  

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic knowledge of verbs, moods, and tenses*  

---

## 1. What is the Present Active Imperative?

The **imperative mood** expresses a command, request, or invitation.  
When we speak of the **present** imperative we are giving a command that is meant to be carried out **now** (or repeatedly in the present).  

- **Active voice** – the subject of the verb performs the action.  
- **Present tense** – the action is contemporaneous with the moment of speaking.  

In Ancient Greek the present active imperative has two regular forms:

| Person | Regular ending | Example with *λύω* “to loose” |
|--------|----------------|--------------------------------|
| 2nd sg | **‑ε** (or **‑ον** for some contract verbs) | λῦε (or λῦον) “loose!” |
| 2nd pl | **‑ετε** | λύετε “loosen!” |

> **Why “‑ω” appears in the heading**  
> The label “present, imperative, active (ω)” comes from the way the **present stem** is built.  
> The present stem of a regular (thematic) verb ends in the vowel **‑ω** (the “thematic vowel”). This vowel is present in the indicative (ἔγωγ **‑ω**) and also underlies the formation of the imperative.  
> Therefore we will keep the **‑ω** in mind when we derive the imperative endings.

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1. Regular Thematic Verbs (‑ω verbs)

These verbs have a present stem ending in **‑ω** plus the thematic vowel **‑ο‑** (or **‑ε‑** after contraction).  

| Person | Imperative ending | Full form (stem + ending) | Example with *γράφω* “to write” |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| 2nd sg | **‑ε** (or **‑ον** after contraction) | γράφ‑ε | γράφε! “write!” |
| 2nd pl | **‑ετε** | γράφ‑ετε | γράφετε! “write!” |

*If the verb is a contract verb (‑άω, ‑έω, ‑όω), the **‑ε** becomes **‑α**, **‑ε**, **‑ο** respectively, and the 2nd sg often appears as **‑α**, **‑ε**, **‑ο** (or the longer **‑ατε**, **‑ετε**, **‑ετε** in the plural).*

### 2.2. Contract Verbs  

| Verb type | Present stem | 2nd sg Imperative | 2nd pl Imperative |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **‑άω** (e.g., *φιλέω* → *φιλεῖ* → *φιλοῦ*) | ‑α‑ | **‑α** (or **‑ατε** in 2nd pl) | **‑ατε** |
| **‑έω** (e.g., *ποιέω* → *ποιεῖ* → *ποιεῖν*) | ‑ε‑ | **‑ε** | **‑ετε** |
| **‑όω** (e.g., *διδάσκω* → *διδάσκει* → *διδάσκειν*) | ‑ο‑ | **‑ο** | **‑ετε** |

> **Note:** Many contract verbs have a **long vowel** in the 2nd sg imperative (e.g., *διδάσκει* → *διδάσκε* → *διδάσκε!*) but the pattern above captures the most common endings.

### 2.3. Irregular Verbs  

| Verb | 2nd sg Imperative | 2nd pl Imperative | Remarks |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|---------|
| **εἰμί** “to be” | **ἔστω** | **ἔστωσαν** | Rarely used; mostly in formal or poetic contexts |
| **ἔχω** “to have, hold” | **ἔχε** | **ἔχετε** | Same pattern as regular verbs |
| **φημί** “to say” | **φῆσον** | **φῆσον** (pl. often **φῆσατε**) | Uses a special stem *φῆσ‑* |
| **ἰέναι** (infinitive of *ἔρχομαι*) | — | — | No present imperative; the verb is defective |

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **A. Use the 2nd person** | The imperative is always addressed to the **addressee** (you). | λῦε “loose!” (you, singular) |
| **B. No 1st‑person or 3rd‑person imperatives** (except in poetry) | Only 2nd sg and 2nd pl are regular. | — |
| **C. The thematic vowel ‑ω disappears** | The **‑ω** of the present stem is dropped before the imperative endings. | γράφ‑ε (not *γράφ‑ω‑ε*) |
| **D. Contract verbs contract further** | After a long vowel, the **‑ε** of the imperative may become **‑α**, **‑ε**, **‑ο**. | φιλεῖ → **φιλε** → **φίλε!** |
| **E. Strong verbs may add a **‑σ** before the ending** | Verbs whose stem ends in a vowel often insert **‑σ** (e.g., *ἔρχομαι* → *ἔρχε*). | ἔρχε! “come!” |
| **F. Negative commands use **μὴ** (present) or **οὐ** (future)** | *μὴ* + imperative for present commands; *οὐ* + future imperative for future commands. | μὴ λῦε “don’t loose!” |
| **G. Enclitic particles** | The particle **δή** (emphatic) or **γε** (restrictive) can follow the imperative. | γράφε δὴ! “write, indeed!” |
| **H. Irregular stems** | Some verbs have a different stem in the imperative (e.g., *φημί* → *φῆσον*). | φῆσον “say!” |

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Direct commands** – Most common in dialogues, instructions, and legal texts.  
2. **Poetic exhortations** – Often appear with particles **δή**, **γε**, or **ἦ** for emphasis.  
3. **Negative commands** – Use **μὴ** + present imperative (or **οὐ** + future imperative for future‑oriented commands).  
4. **Politeness** – In formal or polite speech, the imperative may be softened by adding **παρακαλῶ** (“I ask”) before the verb: *παρακαλῶ γράφετε* “please write”.  
5. **Epic and Tragic style** – The 2nd sg imperative can appear with a long vowel (e.g., *ἔρχε* instead of *ἔρχου*) for metrical reasons.

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

- **Proto‑Indo‑European roots**: The imperative derives from the PIE *‑si* suffix, which in Greek merged with the present stem vowel **‑ω** to give the characteristic **‑ε/‑ετε** endings.  
- **Thematic vowel –ω**: In early Greek the present stem ended in **‑ο‑** (thematic vowel). The **‑ω** of the 1st‑person singular indicative is a later development; the imperative retains the older stem without the **‑ω**.  
- **Contract verbs**: The contraction of the thematic vowel with the stem vowel (‑α‑, ‑ε‑, ‑ο‑) gave rise to the three contract classes (‑άω, ‑έω, ‑όω). Their imperatives show the same contraction pattern, which is why we see forms like **‑α**, **‑ε**, **‑ο**.  
- **Negative particle μὴ**: Originally a conjunction meaning “that not”, it became the standard negator of present imperatives in Classical Greek, replacing the older *οὐ* for present commands.
