# Lesson: The Aorist Passive Participle (‑θ‑ + ‑ω, ‑α, ‑ον)

**Target level:** Beginner‑Intermediate  
**Prerequisites:** Greek alphabet, basic verb forms (present, aorist, infinitive), noun/adjective declension.

---

## 1. What is the Aorist Passive Participle?

A **participle** is a verb‑form that functions like an adjective: it can describe a noun and agree with it in gender, number, and case.  

The **aorist** expresses a simple, completed action (the “plain past”).  

The **passive voice** shows that the subject is acted upon.

Putting these together, the **aorist passive participle** means “the one who has been … (verb)”. It describes a noun that has undergone the action in the past, without indicating *when* the action happened—only that it is completed.

> **λύθων ἀνὴρ** – “the man who has been loosed / the man who was freed”

---

## 2. Morphology – How the Form Is Built

| Step | Description | Example (λύω “to loose”) |
|------|-------------|--------------------------|
| **Verb stem** | Take the **aorist passive stem** (the stem used for the aorist passive forms). For regular verbs this is the **aorist passive root** + **‑θ‑**. | λυ‑ → λυ‑**θ**‑ |
| **Suffix** | Insert the **‑θ‑** (the aorist passive marker). | λυ‑**θ**‑ |
| **Adjectival endings** | Add the standard **adjective endings** of the **first/second declension** (‑ων, ‑ουσα, ‑ον, etc.). | λυ‑θ‑**ων** (masc. nom. sg.) |
| **Result** | A full participial form that declines like an adjective. | λυθὼς, λυθεῖσα, λυθόν, … |

### 2.1. The Aorist Passive Stem

1. **Regular verbs** – take the aorist passive root (often the same as the aorist active root) and add **‑θ‑**.  
   *λύω* → aorist passive *λύθην* → stem **λυθ‑**.

2. **Verbs with a special passive stem** – some verbs have a different passive stem (often ending in **‑σ‑** or **‑ψ‑**).  
   *πίπτω* → aorist passive *πίπτω* (same) → stem **πτι‑** → participle **πτιθ‑** (rare, see exceptions).

3. **Irregular verbs** – a few verbs have irregular participles (e.g., *ἔρχομαι* → *ἐληλυθὼς* is perfect, not aorist).

---

## 3. Full Paradigm

The aorist passive participle follows the **first/second declension adjective** pattern. Below is the complete paradigm for the verb **λύω** (“to loose”).

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Singular** | | | |
| Nominative | λυθ**ὼς** | λυθ**εῖσα** | λυθ**όν** |
| Genitive | λυθ**οῦ** | λυθ**εῖσας** | λυθ**οῦ** |
| Dative | λυθ**ῷ** | λυθ**εῖσῃ** | λυθ**ῷ** |
| Accusative | λυθ**όντα** | λυθ**εῖσαν** | λυθ**όν** |
| Vocative* | λυθ**ὼ** | λυθ**εῖσα** | λυθ**όν** |
| **Plural** | | | |
| Nominative | λυθ**ότες** | λυθ**εῖσαι** | λυθ**ότα** |
| Genitive | λυθ**ότων** | λυθ**εῖσῶν** | λυθ**ότων** |
| Dative | λυθ**οῖς** | λυθ**εῖσαις** | λυθ**οῖς** |
| Accusative | λυθ**οὺς** | λυθ**εῖσας** | λυθ**ότα** |
| Vocative | λυθ**ότε** | λυθ**εῖσαι** | λυθ**ότα** |

\*The vocative is rarely used with participles, but the form exists for completeness.

**Key points to notice**

* The **‑θ‑** appears in the stem (λυθ‑).  
* The endings are identical to those of a regular adjective.  
* The participle can be **masc., fem., neut.** and **sing./plur.** – it must match the noun it modifies.

## 4. Key Rules & Common Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Use the aorist passive stem** | Find the aorist passive form (usually *‑θην*). Drop the *‑ην* and add **‑θ‑**. | *λύθην* → stem **λυθ‑** → λυθὼς |
| **2. Contract verbs** | For verbs whose stem ends in a vowel, the **‑θ‑** contracts with the vowel. | *δέχομαι* → aorist passive *δέχθην* → stem **δεχθ‑** → participle **δεχθὼς** |
| **3. Verbs with a **‑σ‑** or **‑ψ‑** in the passive stem** | The **‑σ‑** or **‑ψ‑** remains before **‑θ‑**. | *πίπτω* → aorist passive *πίπτω* (stem **πτι‑**) → participle **πτιθ‑** (rare, usually avoided) |
| **4. Irregular participles** | Some verbs have suppletive or irregular participles (e.g., *ἔρχομαι* → *ἐληλυθὼς* is perfect, not aorist). Use the lexical entry. |
| **5. No augment** | Unlike the aorist passive indicative (*λύθην*), the participle **does not take the augment** (the “ε‑” that marks past time). | *λύθην* (with augment) → λυθὼς (no augment). |
| **6. Agreement** | The participle must agree **exactly** with the noun it modifies in gender, number, and case. | λυθὼς **ἀνὴρ** (masc. nom. sg.) vs. λυθεῖσα **γυνή** (fem. nom. sg.). |
| **7. Use with the verb *εἰμί*** | When the participle functions as a predicate adjective, it is often combined with *εἰμί* (to be). | ὁ ἀνὴρ **λύθων** ἐστίν – “the man is (the one who) has been freed.” |

### Common Exceptions

| Verb | Irregular aorist passive participle | Note |
|------|--------------------------------------|------|
| *ἔρχομαι* | **ἐληλυθὼς** (perfect) | No true aorist passive participle; use perfect. |
| *γίγνομαι* | **γενόμενος** (aorist middle) | Passive meaning expressed by middle. |
| *ὁράω* | **ὁρᾶσθαι** (infinitive) – participle **ὁρᾶσθαι** does not exist; use **ὁρᾶσθαι** periphrastically. |

## 6. Usage & Context

1. **Descriptive (adjectival) use** – Most common. The participle directly modifies a noun.  
   *λυθὼν ἀνὴρ* = “the man who has been freed”.

2. **Predicate with *εἰμί*** – When the participle functions as a predicate adjective.  
   *ὁ ἀνὴρ λυθὼς ἐστίν* – “the man is (the one who) has been freed”.

3. **Temporal nuance** – The aorist passive participle stresses **completion**, not duration. It can be used where English would use a perfect passive (“has been …”) or a simple past passive (“was …”).

4. **With verbs of perception or causation** – The participle can appear after verbs like *ὁρῶ* (I see) or *ἀκούω* (I hear).  
   *ὁρῶ τὸν λυθόντα ἄνθρωπον* – “I see the man who has been freed.”

5. **In indirect speech** – The participle can replace a finite verb in a relative clause.  
   *ἔφη τὸν ἀνθρώπον λυθόντα* – “He said that the man had been freed.”

6. **Stylistic register** – The aorist passive participle is common in **classical prose** and **poetry** for compact description. In later Koine, the periphrastic *ἐγένετο* + participle becomes more frequent.

---

## 7. Historical Development & Etymology

| Period | Development |
|--------|--------------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European** | The participial suffix *‑t‑* (later *‑θ‑* in Greek) was used to form past participles in many daughter languages. |
| **Mycenaean Greek** | Early evidence of the *‑θ‑* participle appears in Linear B tablets (e.g., *ka‑to‑* “having been cut”). |
| **Classical Greek** | The aorist passive participle becomes a regular, productive adjective
