# Lesson: The Present‑Indicative‑Active (μι) in Ancient Greek  

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic idea of verbs, person‑number agreement*  

---

## 1. What the Present‑Indicative‑Active Is  

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Present** | The action is happening **now** or habitually, or it expresses a timeless/general truth. |
| **Indicative** | The “statement” mood – used for ordinary factual or neutral statements (as opposed to subjunctive, optative, imperative, etc.). |
| **Active** | The subject **does** the action (as opposed to middle or passive). |
| **(μι)** | The **first‑person singular** present‑indicative‑active ending for **‑μι**‑type verbs (the “contract” verbs that end in **‑μι** in the infinitive). |

Thus **μι** is the form you will see for verbs such as *λῡ́ω* → *λῡ́ω* (I loose), *φιλέω* → *φιλέω* (I love), *ἔρχομαι* → *ἔρχομαι* (I come), etc. The paradigm built from this ending is the **present‑indicative‑active** system.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1 The Regular Paradigm (‑μι verbs)

The present‑indicative‑active of a regular **‑μι** verb follows this pattern:

| Person | Singular | Dual* | Plural |
|--------|----------|-------|--------|
| 1st | **‑μι** | **‑μεν** | **‑μεν** |
| 2nd | **‑ς** | **‑τε** | **‑τε** |
| 3rd | **‑σι(ν)** | **‑τον** | **‑σι(ν)** |

\*The dual is rarely used in later Greek but is retained in the paradigm for completeness.

**Full paradigm example:** *φιλέω* “to love”

|          | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|----------|----------|------|--------|
| 1st      | **φιλέω** | **φιλέμεν** | **φιλέμεν** |
| 2nd      | **φιλέεις** | **φιλέετε** | **φιλέετε** |
| 3rd      | **φιλέει** | **φιλέετον** | **φιλέουσι(ν)** |

> **Note:** The 3rd‑person plural ending **‑ουσι(ν)** is the “contract” form that replaces the expected **‑σι** after a long vowel or diphthong (here *ε* contracts to *ε* + *ου*).  

### 2.2 Common Variations  

| Verb type | Typical 1st‑sg. ending | Example | Remarks |
|-----------|-----------------------|---------|---------|
| **‑μι** (regular) | **‑μι** | *λῡ́ω* → **λῡ́ω** | Most common pattern |
| **‑εω** (contract) | **‑ω** | *φιλέω* → **φιλέω** | The **‑εω** infinitive contracts to **‑ω** in the present. |
| **‑αω** (contract) | **‑α** | *διδάσκαω* → **διδάσκα** (rare) | Very few verbs; often become **‑ω** after contraction. |
| **Irregular** | varies | *εἰμί* → **εἰμί** (to be) | The verb *εἰμί* has its own paradigm (see § 3). |

---

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Contract verbs** | Verbs whose infinitive ends in **‑εω**, **‑αω**, or **‑οω** contract in the present. The vowel of the stem lengthens and the ending **‑ω** appears in 1st‑sg. | *φιλέω* → **φιλέω**, *παιδέω* → **παιδέω** |
| **2. Long‑vowel stems** | If the stem ends in a long vowel or diphthong, the 3rd‑person plural ending becomes **‑ουσι(ν)** (instead of **‑σι(ν)**). | *ποιέω* → **ποιοῦσι(ν)** |
| **3. The verb εἰμί** | Completely irregular; its present‑indicative‑active forms are: **εἰμί, εἶ, ἐστί(ν), ἐσμέν, ἐστέ, εἰσίν**. | — |
| **4. The verb ἔχω** | Though its infinitive ends in **‑εῖν**, the present uses **‑ω** in 1st‑sg.: **ἔχω**. The rest of the paradigm follows the regular pattern. | — |
| **5. Dual forms** | The dual (‑τον, ‑τε) survives mainly in poetry and early prose; in most later texts they are replaced by the plural. | — |
| **6. Spurious diphthongs** | In some verbs, **ει** and **ου** may be “spurious” diphthongs (historically from a vowel + *i* or *u*). They behave like long vowels for the purpose of the **‑ουσι** ending. | *γίγνομαι* → **γίγνονται** (spurious diphthong *οι*). |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (present‑indicative‑active) | Transliteration | English |
|-----------------------------------|----------------|---------|
| **φιλέω τὸν φίλον.** | *phileō ton philon.* | I love the friend. |
| **λῡ́ει τὸν δεσμὸν.** | *lūei ton desmon.* | He/She looses the chain. |
| **παιδέω τὰ παιδία.** | *paideō ta paidia.* | I teach the children. |
| **ποιοῦσι τὰ ἔργα.** | *poiousi ta erga.* | They are doing the works. |
| **εἰμι ἐν τῇ οἰκίᾳ.** | *eimi en tē oikia.* | I am in the house. |

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Simple present** – Describes an action occurring **right now**:  
   *ἔρχομαι* → *ἔρχομαι* “I am coming.”  

2. **Habitual / repeated action** – Indicates a regular or repeated activity:  
   *παιδέω* → *παιδέω* “I teach (as a profession).”  

3. **General truths** – Used for statements that are always true:  
   *ὁ ἥλιος φαίνει* – “The sun shines.”  

4. **Narrative present** – In literary prose (especially in drama and historiography) the present can be used to give a vivid, “live” description of past events.  

5. **Contrast with other moods** – The indicative is the “default” mood; when you need a command, wish, or condition, you will switch to the imperative, optative, or subjunctive, respectively.

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** | The present‑indicative‑active derives from the PIE *‑mi* suffix, which marked the first‑person singular present indicative. The same suffix appears in Sanskrit (*‑mi*), Latin (*‑o*), and Old English (*‑e*). |
| **Evolution of the **‑μι** ending** | In early Greek the ending **‑μι** was used for all persons (e.g., *λύ̄μι, λύ̄ς, λύ̄σι*). By the Classical period, the system had split into the more elaborate set shown above, with **‑μι** surviving only in the 1st‑sg. |
| **Contract verbs** | The contraction of **‑εω**, **‑αω**, **‑οω** stems reflects a phonological process where a short vowel plus *w* (digamma) merged, giving the long vowel or diphthong seen in the present. This is why *φιλέω* (from *φιλε‑ε‑ω*) becomes **φιλέω**. |
| **Irregular verbs** | *εἰμί* preserves an ancient suppletive paradigm (different roots for different tenses) and is the only verb that keeps the original PIE *‑mi* in the 1st‑sg. |
| **Loss of the dual** | The dual forms (‑τον, ‑τε) gradually fell out of everyday speech after the Classical period, surviving mainly in poetry and formal prose. |
