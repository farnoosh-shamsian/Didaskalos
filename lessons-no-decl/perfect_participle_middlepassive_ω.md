# Lesson: The Perfect Middle/Passive Participle (‑ω)  

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic verb endings, the concept of voice and tense*  

---

## 1. What is the Perfect Middle/Passive Participle?

In Ancient Greek the **perfect** expresses a **completed action whose result still holds** (e.g., “I have written – the writing exists”).  
A **participle** is a verbal adjective: it has the meaning of a verb but behaves like an adjective, agreeing with a noun in case, number, and gender.

The **perfect middle/passive participle** therefore:

| Feature | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| **Tense** | Perfect – the action is completed and its result remains. |
| **Voice** | Middle or Passive – the subject is either acting on itself/for its own benefit (middle) or is acted upon (passive). |
| **Form** | Built from the perfect stem + the participial suffix **‑ω** (more precisely **‑ώς** in the masculine nominative singular) and the regular adjective endings. |
| **Function** | Used like any adjective: as a predicate adjective, attributive modifier, or in a genitive of description. |

> **Key idea:** The perfect middle/passive participle describes a *state* that results from a past action.  
> Example: *γεγραμμένος* “having been written / written (and still existing)”.

---

## 2. Formation: Stem + Suffix + Adjective Endings  

### 2.1 The Perfect Stem  

1. **Regular verbs**: take the **perfect stem** (the one that appears in the perfect indicative, e.g., *γέγραψ‑* from *γράφω*).  
2. **Irregular verbs** may have a vowel change or a suppletive stem (e.g., *ἔλαβ‑* → *εληλυθ‑*).  

### 2.2 The Participial Suffix  

| Voice | Suffix (masc. nom. sg.) |
|-------|------------------------|
| **Middle/Passive** | **‑υῖα / ‑υῖον** (older literary) **or** **‑μένος** (the more common “‑μένος” formation) |
| **Active** | **‑ώς** (not the focus of this lesson) |

The “‑ω” you see in the textbook heading refers to the **‑ω‑** element of the perfect stem (e.g., *γε‑* + *‑γραμ‑* + *‑‑*). The participial ending that follows is **‑μένος** (or the rarer **‑υῖα/‑υῖον**).

### 2.3 Full Paradigm (using the verb **γράφω** “to write”)  

| Case | Masculine Singular | Feminine Singular | Neuter Singular | Masculine Plural | Feminine Plural | Neuter Plural |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|----------------|------------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Nominative** | **γεγραμμένος** | **γεγραμμένη** | **γεγραμμένον** | **γεγραμμένοι** | **γεγραμμέναι** | **γεγραμμένα** |
| **Genitive**   | γεγραμμένου | γεγραμμένης | γεγραμμένου | γεγραμμένων | γεγραμμένων | γεγραμμένων |
| **Dative**     | γεγραμμένῳ | γεγραμμένῃ | γεγραμμένῳ | γεγραμμένοις | γεγραμμέναις | γεγραμμένοις |
| **Accusative** | γεγραμμένον | γεγραμμένην | γεγραμμένον | γεγραμμένους | γεγραμμένας | γεγραμμένα |
| **Vocative**   | γεγραμμένε | γεγραμμένη | γεγραμμένον | γεγραμμένοι | γεγραμμέναι | γεγραμμένα |

> The **‑μένος** pattern behaves exactly like a regular **‑ος/‑η/‑ον** adjective.  

### 2.4 The Older **‑υῖα / ‑υῖον** Paradigm (rare, literary)

| Case | Masculine Singular | Feminine Singular | Neuter Singular |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Nominative | **λελυκ‑υῖα** (rare) | **λελυκ‑υῖα** | **λελυκ‑υῖον** |
| … (other cases follow the same pattern as a regular adjective) |

*Because the **‑υῖα/‑υῖον** forms are infrequent, most textbooks teach the **‑μένος** formation as the standard perfect middle/passive participle.*

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **1. Use the perfect stem** | Find the perfect indicative (e.g., *γέγραψα* → stem *γρ‑α‑* → perfect stem *γεγραπ‑* → *γεγραμ‑* after assimilation). |
| **2. Add ‑μένος** | Attach **‑μένος** to the perfect stem. The preceding vowel may contract (e.g., *ε‑* + *‑α* → *‑α*). |
| **3. Agreement** | The participle declines like a regular adjective of the 1st/2nd declension (masc. ‑ος, fem. ‑η/‑α, neut. ‑ον). |
| **4. Middle vs. Passive meaning** | Context decides whether the participle is middle (the subject benefits) or passive (the subject is acted upon). Many participles are ambiguous; the surrounding verb or noun clarifies. |
| **5. Irregular stems** | Some verbs have suppletive perfect stems (e.g., *ἔρχομαι* → perfect *ἦλθον* → participle *ἐληλυθώς* (active) but passive *ἐληλυθὼς* is not used; instead we use *ἐληλυθώς* only in active sense). For the middle/passive we use *ἐληλυθώς* only as active; the passive sense is expressed with *ἐληλυθέν* (aorist) or a different construction. |
| **6. Verbs with a‑thetic perfect** | Verbs like *ἔρχομαι* have a perfect **‑εσ-** form (*ἐλήλυθα*). Their participle is **‑εσ- + ‑μένος** → *ἐληλυθέν* (rare). Usually the **‑μένος** participle is avoided; a periphrastic expression (*ὁ ἐληλυθὼς*) is used. |
| **7. Contracted verbs** | If the perfect stem ends in a long vowel, the **‑μένος** may contract: *πράττω* → perfect *πέπραχα* → participle *πεπραγμένος* (instead of *πραγμένος*). |
| **8. Dual forms** | The dual of the perfect participle exists but is rarely used; it follows the regular dual endings (‑ον, ‑οιν). |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek | Transliteration | English |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| **Ὁ ποιητὴς **γεγραμμένος** ἐν τῇ ἀγορᾷ ἐστίν.** | Ho poiētēs **gegrammenos** en tēi agoraí estín. | The poet **who has been written** (i.e., whose work is written) is in the market. |
| **Ἡ πόλις **γεγυμνασμένη** ἀνδρῶν ἐστίν.** | Hē polis **gegymnasmenē** andrōn estín. | The city **having been trained** (i.e., whose citizens are trained) is a city of men. |
| **Τὰ βιβλία **γεγραμμένα** ἐν τῷ ταμείῳ εἰσίν.** | Ta biblia **gegrammena** en tōi tameíōi eisin. | The books **that have been written** are in the store. |
| **Ὁ ἀνὴρ **γεγραμμένος** ἐν τῇ ἐπιστολῇ ἀντιδρᾷ.** | Ho anēr **gegrammenos** en tēi epistolei antidrāi. | The man **who has been written about** (i.e., mentioned) replies in the letter. |

*Notice how the participle agrees with the noun it modifies (gender, number, case).*

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Attributive** – placed directly before or after a noun, often with the article:  
   *ὁ **γεγραμμένος** νόμος* “the law that has been written”.  

2. **Predicate** – after a copular verb (εἰμί, γίγνεσθαι, φαίνεσθαι):  
   *Ἡ πόλις **γεγραμμένη** ἐστίν* “The city is written (i.e., recorded)”.  

3. **Genitive of description** – after nouns of abstract meaning:  
   *ἡ μνήμη τῶν **γεγραμμένων** ἔργων* “the
