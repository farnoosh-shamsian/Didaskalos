# Lesson: The Imperfect Indicative Active of **‑μι** Verbs  
*(Ancient Greek – Beginner‑Intermediate)*  

---

## 1. What the Imperfect Indicative Active Is  

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Imperfect** | A past‑time tense that describes an action or state that was **continuous, repeated, or incomplete** in the past. |
| **Indicative** | The “real” mood – statements of fact (as opposed to subjunctive, optative, etc.). |
| **Active** | The subject of the verb performs the action (as opposed to middle or passive). |
| **‑μι** | A small class of verbs whose **present‑stem ends in ‑μι** (e.g., *φημί* “to say”, *δείκνυμι* “to show”). In the imperfect they behave like the regular **‑ω** verbs, but a few have irregular forms. |

The imperfect therefore tells us **what someone was doing, what was happening, or what a state was like** at some past moment, without implying that the action was finished.

> **English equivalents** – “was/were …‑ing”, “used to …”, “did … (repeatedly)”.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1 General Formation  

1. **Augment** – add **ἐ‑** (or **ἠ‑** after a vowel) to the beginning of the verb.  
2. **Stem** – take the **present‑stem** (the part before ‑μι).  
3. **Imperfect marker** – insert **‑ον‑** after the stem.  
4. **Personal endings** – attach the active imperfect endings (see table).  

**Formula:**  

```
ἐ‑ + (present‑stem) + ‑ον‑ + personal ending
```

### 2.2 Active Imperfect Endings  

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st    | ‑ν       | ‑μεν   |
| 2nd    | ‑ς       | ‑τε    |
| 3rd    | (zero)   | ‑ν     |

These are the same endings used for regular **‑ω** verbs.

### 2.3 Paradigm for a Regular **‑μι** Verb  
**Verb:** *δείκνυμι* “to show” (present‑stem *δείκνυ‑*)  

|          | 1st sg | 2nd sg | 3rd sg | 1st pl | 2nd pl | 3rd pl |
|----------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| **Imperfect** | ἐδείκνυον | ἐδείκνυες | ἐδείκνυε(ν) | ἐδείκνυον | ἐδείκνυτε | ἐδείκνυον |

*Notice the augment ἐ‑ and the marker ‑ον‑ before the endings.*

### 2.4 Irregular **‑μι** Verb: *φημί* “to say”  

*φημί* does **not** follow the regular pattern; its imperfect forms are lexicalised.

|          | 1st sg | 2nd sg | 3rd sg | 1st pl | 2nd pl | 3rd pl |
|----------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| **Imperfect** | ἔφην | ἔφης | ἔφη | ἔφηνμεν | ἔφητε | ἔφασι(ν) |

*The forms look like a mixture of the regular endings with a contracted stem (φ‑) and a special 3rd‑pl. ending ‑σι(ν).*

---

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **Augment** | All imperfect forms receive **ἐ‑** (or **ἠ‑** after a vowel). No augment in the perfect, aorist, or future. |
| **‑ον‑ marker** | The **‑ον‑** element is obligatory for regular **‑μι** verbs. It is the same element found in **‑ω** verbs. |
| **Contracted stems** | If the present‑stem ends in a vowel, the augment may cause contraction (e.g., *ἔ‑* + *α* → *ἠ‑*). |
| **Irregular verbs** | *φημί* and a handful of others (*ἱκνέω?* – actually middle) have irregular imperfects; they must be memorised. |
| **Spelling of 3rd‑sg** | The 3rd‑sg ending is **‑ε(ν)**; the **ν** is optional in prose (often omitted). |
| **Dialectal variation** | In some Doric texts the augment may be absent (the “unaugmented imperfect”), but in Attic‑standard Greek it is always present. |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (Imperfect) | Transliteration | English |

| ἐδείκνυον τὸν δρόμον τῷ ἀνθρώπῳ. | *edéiknyon tòn drómon tôi anthrṓpō.* | I was showing the road to the man. |

| ἔφασι ὅτι ἥξουσιν. | *éphasi hóti hḗxousin.* | They were saying that they would come. |

## 5. Notes on Usage & Context  

1. **Descriptive past** – Use the imperfect for background information, habitual actions, or ongoing states:  
   *Ἐδείκνυον τὰς ἀσκήσεις ἕκαστον πρωί* – “I used to show the exercises every morning.”  

2. **Contrast with Aorist** – The aorist *ἔδειξα* would mean “I showed (once, completed)”, whereas the imperfect *ἐδείκνυον* stresses the **duration** or **repetition**.  

3. **Narrative style** – In classical narrative (e.g., Herodotus, Thucydides) the imperfect often sets the scene:  
   *Ἐδείκνυεν ὁ ἀνήρ τὸν χάρτην, ὅτε ἦλθον οἱ ξένοι.* – “The man was showing the map when the strangers arrived.”  

4. **Verb *φημί*** – Because *φημί* is the main verb for “to say”, its imperfect is extremely common in indirect speech and reported statements.  

5. **Middle/Passive** – The imperfect also exists in middle and passive voices (e.g., *ἐδείχθην* “I was shown”), but this lesson focuses on the **active** voice only.

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** | The **‑μι** suffix goes back to PIE *‑mi* “I”. It originally marked the **first‑person singular present**. Over time Greek re‑analysed it as part of the verb stem. |
| **Old Greek** | In Homeric Greek the imperfect of *‑μι* verbs often appears **without the augment** (e.g., *ἔδειξα* vs. *ἐδείκνυον*). The augment became regular in Classical Attic. |
| **Phonological change** | The **‑ον‑** element is the same as the **‑ον‑** of the imperfect of **‑ω** verbs; it reflects an old past‑tense suffix *‑on‑* that survived in Greek. |
| **Irregularities** | *φημί* preserves an older, contracted form (*φ‑* + *‑ην*), likely derived from a Proto‑Greek *\*bʰeh₁‑* “to speak”. Its irregular imperfect (*ἔφην, ἔφη*) is a relic of a pre‑Greek verbal paradigm that survived only in a few high‑frequency verbs. |
| **Later Greek** | In Koine and Byzantine Greek the imperfect gradually fell out of everyday speech, being replaced by periphrastic constructions (*ἤμην λέγων*). However, the forms survive in the New Testament and in later literary Greek. |

## 7. Quick Reference Summary  

| Verb | Present Stem | Imperfect (Active Indicative) |
|------|--------------|------------------------------|
| **δείκνυμι** | δείκνυ‑ | ἐδείκνυον, ἐδείκνυες, ἐδείκνυε(ν), ἐδείκνυμεν, ἐδείκνυτε, ἐδείκνυον |
| **φημί** (irregular) | φ‑ | ἔφην, ἔφης, ἔφη, ἔφηνμεν, ἔφητε, ἔφασι(ν) |

Remember: **augment + stem + ‑ον‑ + ending** (regular) → continuous past action.
